# Program Oral Interpretation — Judge Prep Guide

> **High School Event** | Max 10 min (+0:30 grace) | At least 2 of 3 genres | Manuscript required | No props or costumes except the manuscript

## Event Overview

In Program Oral Interpretation (POI), students build a **program** of literature woven together around a single unifying theme or argument. The program draws from **at least two of the three genres** — prose, poetry, and drama (plays) — with all three encouraged. With a spotlight on argumentation and performative range, the performer is expected to portray **multiple characters** and make an interesting argument that each piece of literature supports in a different way. The literature is cut and threaded together throughout the performance rather than read one full piece at a time. A **manuscript** (typically a binder) must be used, and no props or costumes are permitted except for that manuscript.

**Structure** (a typical interp arc; not rigid):
- **Teaser** (0:00–1:30): Previews the topic and mood of the program
- **Introduction** (1:30–3:00): Explains the purpose/relevance, then names the title and author of each piece used
- **Exposition** (3:00–3:30): Introduces characters and setting
- **Inciting Incident** (3:30–4:00): Sends the conflict into motion
- **Rising Action** (4:00–7:30): Complicates the conflict
- **Climax** (7:30–8:30): Emotional peak of the performance
- **Falling Action** (8:30–9:30): Resolves the conflict

**Key rules to know:**
- Program must contain **at least two of the three genres** (prose, poetry, drama); all three are encouraged
- Time limit: **10 minutes, with a 30-second grace period**
- A **manuscript must be used**; the performer must stay in control of it throughout the entire performance
- No props or costumes except the manuscript (which may be used as a prop — e.g., a binder mimicking a laptop — as long as it stays controlled)
- Students portray **multiple characters**
- The **introduction is written by the student** and must state the purpose plus the titles and authors used
- Literature selection should suit the student's age, maturity, and school/team/coach standards

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Theme & Argument
**What to evaluate:** Is there a clear, meaningful theme or argument tying the program together? Does each piece genuinely support that thread, and do you leave understanding what the performer wanted to say?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Sharp, meaningful argument; every piece advances it from a different angle; the program clearly says something |
| **Good** | A theme is present and mostly holds; one or two pieces feel loosely connected; argument is implied more than made |
| **Needs Work** | Theme is vague or just a topic, not an argument; pieces feel assembled rather than threaded; unclear takeaway |

---

### 2. Program Selection & Use of Multiple Genres
**What to evaluate:** Did the student select strong, appropriate literature spanning at least two genres? Is each genre meaningfully present and balanced, or does one piece dominate while another is an afterthought?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Diverse, well-chosen literature; genres are balanced and present throughout; each piece distinct in tone and length |
| **Good** | Meets the genre requirement; selections work but balance is uneven, or a genre appears only briefly |
| **Needs Work** | One piece dominates (e.g., eight minutes of one play, a poem tacked on); genre minimum barely met; weak or ill-fitting selections |

---

### 3. Cutting, Transitions & Weaving
**What to evaluate:** Is the literature cut and threaded so the program flows as one piece? Are transitions between pieces purposeful and clear? Does a thread piece or recurring character help carry the audience through?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Seamless weaving; pieces interrupt and answer one another with intention; clear thread; transitions sharpen the argument |
| **Good** | Generally smooth; most transitions land but a few feel abrupt or like simple stacking of pieces |
| **Needs Work** | Pieces performed in sequence rather than woven; jarring or unmotivated transitions; no thread for the audience to follow |

---

### 4. Use of the Binder / Manuscript
**What to evaluate:** Is the manuscript used purposefully and kept under control throughout? Do page turns mark transitions cleanly, or does the binder distract, droop, or interrupt the performance?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Manuscript fully controlled and integrated; page turns signal transitions intentionally; sometimes used as a prop with purpose |
| **Good** | Manuscript handled competently; occasional fumble, glance-down, or page turn that breaks the moment |
| **Needs Work** | Binder distracts — dropped focus, loss of control, awkward handling; reliance on reading rather than performing |

---

### 5. Characterization & Vocal/Physical Performance
**What to evaluate:** Are the multiple characters distinct and believable? Do vocal variety (rate, pitch, volume, pause) and physical choices (blocking, gesture, facial expression) serve the literature and distinguish prose, poetry, and drama?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Vivid, distinct characters; controlled vocal and physical range; each genre performed with its own feel; emotional depth and breadth |
| **Good** | Characters mostly distinguishable; some vocal/physical variety; occasional blurring between characters or flat stretches |
| **Needs Work** | Characters hard to tell apart; monotone or rigid delivery; little distinction between genres; minimal emotional range |

---

### 6. Introduction
**What to evaluate:** Does the introduction explain the program's purpose/relevance and name the title and author of each piece? Does it frame the argument and draw the audience in?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Purpose and stakes are clear; all titles and authors named smoothly; intro frames the argument and engages immediately |
| **Good** | Required info present but intro feels formulaic, listy, or rushed; relevance stated but not compelling |
| **Needs Work** | Missing titles/authors or unclear purpose; intro disconnected from the program or fails to set up the argument |

---

### 7. Overall Effect / Impact
**What to evaluate:** Did the program work as a unified whole? Did the argument land, and did the performance move you?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Cohesive, memorable, emotionally resonant; the argument stayed with you; performance felt complete and intentional |
| **Good** | Solid and coherent; argument communicated; some emotional connection but uneven impact |
| **Needs Work** | Felt like separate pieces rather than a program; argument unclear; performance flat or forgettable |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **Genre balance:** Eight minutes of one play with a poem tacked on at the end is not a strong program. Is each genre present *throughout*, not just checked off?
- **Is it a program or a stack?** Strong POI weaves pieces together. Watch for literature simply performed back-to-back with no threading.
- **Manuscript control:** The binder must stay controlled the entire time. Note loss of control, dropped focus, or over-reliance on reading.
- **Distinct characters:** The performer should portray multiple characters. Are they distinguishable, or do they blur together?
- **Theme vs. topic:** A topic ("immigration") is not an argument. Look for a clear point of view that the literature supports.
- **Introduction completeness:** Does it state the purpose and name the title and author of each piece used?
- **Time violations:** Over 10 minutes plus the 30-second grace is a rules violation; note it on the ballot.
- **Appropriateness:** Flag content beyond the student's apparent maturity or your league's standards.

## Sample Judge Comments

### Theme & Argument

**Strong:**
- crystal-clear argument
- every piece earned its place
- distinct angle per piece
- clear takeaway

**Average:**
- argument implied, not stated
- some pieces loosely connected

**Needs Work:**
- reads as topic, not argument
- unclear what you wanted to say
- decide on your central claim
- let claim filter each piece

---

### Program Selection & Use of Multiple Genres

**Strong:**
- diverse, well-balanced selections
- each genre present throughout
- every piece distinct in feel

**Average:**
- genre requirement met
- uneven balance, one genre brief
- weave that genre in earlier

**Needs Work:**
- one piece dominates time
- second genre only briefly present
- give each genre real space
- let genres speak to one another

---

### Cutting, Transitions & Weaving

**Strong:**
- seamless weaving
- pieces answer each other
- felt like one purpose-built work
- clear thread character to follow

**Average:**
- mostly flowed
- a few transitions felt abrupt
- find a line to hand off pieces

**Needs Work:**
- pieces performed in sequence
- not woven together
- interleave literature into pages
- give audience a thread to hold

---

### Use of the Binder / Manuscript

**Strong:**
- excellent manuscript control
- page turns mark transitions cleanly
- binder used as prop with intention
- never pulled focus

**Average:**
- handled well overall
- a page turn or glance interrupted
- smooth out those moments

**Needs Work:**
- binder distracted at several points
- dropped focus, loss of control
- practice until it's second nature
- must stay controlled throughout

---

### Characterization & Vocal/Physical Performance

**Strong:**
- vivid, instantly distinct characters
- varied voices, postures, rhythms
- each genre its own feel
- wide emotional range, controlled

**Average:**
- most characters distinguishable
- a few blurred when pace picked up
- lock in signatures per character
- vary delivery across genres

**Needs Work:**
- hard to tell characters apart
- delivery stayed level throughout
- build distinct voices and choices
- let each genre sound different

---

### Introduction

**Strong:**
- purpose and stakes clear
- all titles and authors named smoothly
- framed the argument up front

**Average:**
- required info covered
- felt like a list, not a hook
- state why the program matters

**Needs Work:**
- missing titles/authors
- unclear program purpose
- credit each piece used
- state why the argument matters

---

### Overall Effect / Impact

**Strong:**
- cohesive, memorable program
- everything served one argument
- complete and intentional throughout
- stayed with me

**Average:**
- solid with clear strengths
- argument came through
- tighten the peak moments

**Needs Work:**
- pieces not yet one program
- foundation is here
- let argument drive every choice
- start from your central claim

---

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] At least two of three genres (prose, poetry, drama) present and balanced throughout
- [ ] Clear unifying theme / argument supported by each piece
- [ ] Literature woven together (not performed back-to-back); a thread to follow
- [ ] Manuscript used and kept under control the entire performance
- [ ] No props or costumes except the manuscript
- [ ] Multiple distinct, believable characters
- [ ] Vocal and physical variety; each genre has its own feel
- [ ] Introduction states purpose and names each title and author
- [ ] Within 10-minute limit (plus 30-second grace)
