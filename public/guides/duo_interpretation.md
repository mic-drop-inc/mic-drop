# Duo Interpretation (DUO) — Judge Prep Guide

> **High School & Middle School** | Max 10 minutes | Two performers | No props or costumes | Off-stage focus | Published work

## Event Overview

Duo Interpretation features **two students** performing a cutting from a published work together. They portray characters, narrate, and explore a dramatic or comedic story as a team. Unlike solo interpretation events, DUO is fundamentally about **relationship** — the interplay between performers is as important as individual skill.

**Key distinguishing rules:**
- Off-stage focus: performers look at spots in the air, never directly at each other (even during dialogue)
- No physical contact between performers
- No props or costumes
- Both performers must be from the same school
- The same story arc applies: Teaser → Intro → Exposition → Inciting Incident → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action

**DUO-specific components:**
- **Cutting**: Quality and appropriateness of selected material
- **Characterization**: Individual character development
- **Relationship**: The dynamic between the two performers and their characters
- **Blocking**: Spatial storytelling and off-stage focus
- **Introduction**: Written and co-delivered by the performers

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Literature Selection & Cutting Quality
**What to evaluate:** Is the source material appropriate and well-suited to DUO format? Does the cutting work as a story with a clear arc, and does it require/benefit from two performers?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Published work with dramatic/comedic merit; cutting requires two performers to tell it fully; clear arc; theme is meaningful |
| **Good** | Solid selection; some moments feel forced into the two-person format |
| **Needs Work** | Material doesn't benefit from two performers; cutting lacks arc or clarity; theme is underdeveloped |

---

### 2. Introduction
**What to evaluate:** Does the intro name the work and author? Does it set up the relationship between characters? Is it engaging and co-constructed?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Both performers contribute; names work/author; establishes the relationship at the center of the story; emotionally sets the tone |
| **Good** | Required info present; both performers involved; intro feels rehearsed but not particularly organic |
| **Needs Work** | One performer dominates; missing author or title; intro doesn't contextualize the relationship |

---

### 3. Characterization
**What to evaluate:** Are individual characters distinct and consistent? Do the performers have their own physicality, vocal quality, and emotional logic for each character they play?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Each performer has fully realized, internally consistent characters; transitions clear; characters have depth beyond function |
| **Good** | Characters distinguishable; some consistency gaps across performers |
| **Needs Work** | Characters blend together; unclear who is speaking; characterization is generic |

---

### 4. Relationship / Partnership Dynamic
**What to evaluate:** This is the heart of DUO. Do the performers listen to each other? Is there chemistry? Does the audience feel the relationship between the characters even through off-stage focus?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Performers are genuinely present for each other; relationship is palpable; timing and listening feel natural; one partner's reaction enhances the other's moment |
| **Good** | Solid partnership; some reactive listening; balance mostly maintained |
| **Needs Work** | Performers feel like two soloists sharing a stage; no reactive listening; one partner consistently upstages the other |

---

### 5. Blocking / Physical Performance & Off-Stage Focus
**What to evaluate:** Use of space, consistency of off-stage focus, physical storytelling, and how well the pair uses movement to convey the relationship.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Specific, consistent off-stage focus per character; spatial relationship between performers mirrors the emotional dynamic; transitions and movement are crisp |
| **Good** | Off-stage focus mostly maintained; decent spatial awareness; some blocking feels unmotivated |
| **Needs Work** | Performers look directly at each other during dialogue; unclear off-stage focus; blocking doesn't serve the story |

---

### 6. Balance
**What to evaluate:** Is the performance balanced between partners? DUO should be a collaboration — one performer should not dominate while the other fades.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Both performers have meaningful, roughly equivalent stage time and character investment; neither overshadows the other |
| **Good** | Mostly balanced; one performer slightly more prominent but both contribute |
| **Needs Work** | One performer clearly dominates — other is a sidekick; or the material itself only gives significant role to one |

---

### 7. Overall Effect / Impact
**What to evaluate:** Did the performance work as a unified piece? Did the two-person format enhance the story?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | The pairing felt essential — this story couldn't have been told as well by one person; emotionally resonant and dramatically complete |
| **Good** | Solid performance; story communicated; some moments where the partnership shone |
| **Needs Work** | Performance felt like two solo pieces stitched together; partnership not a strength |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **Off-stage focus violations:** In DUO, partners cannot look directly at each other. Focal points must be "in the air" at specific locations. This is one of the most common errors — watch for it closely.
- **No physical contact:** Performers may not touch during the performance. Note any contact on the ballot.
- **Are both performers from the same school?** Tab should verify, but note if anything seems off.
- **Is one performer carrying the other?** Balance matters — DUO is a partnership.
- **Reactive listening:** The best DUO partnerships react to each other. Watch the performer who is *not* speaking — are they present?
- **Does the cutting require two performers?** If the story would work just as well as a solo, that's a weakness.
- **Published source:** Must come from a published work.
- **Time limit:** 10 minutes maximum.

## Sample Judge Comments

### Literature Selection & Cutting

**Strong:**
- ideal material for DUO
- relationship drives the story
- arc preserved beautifully

**Average:**
- scenes read as parallel monologues
- find material built on relationship

**Needs Work:**
- uneven cutting between roles
- one character carries the weight
- seek equal dramatic investment

---

### Introduction

**Strong:**
- co-constructed intro, both contributed
- named work and author
- established central relationship immediately

**Average:**
- covers the bases
- two voices, not one cohesive voice
- weave your lines together

**Needs Work:**
- only one partner delivered intro
- have both partners participate
- author not named

---

### Characterization

**Strong:**
- distinct, fully realized characters
- consistent from first scene to last
- grounded in real motivation

**Average:**
- primary characters well-drawn
- secondary characters less distinct
- add specificity in narration

**Needs Work:**
- unclear who plays which character
- secondary characters blur together
- commit to a clear vocal signature

---

### Relationship / Partnership Dynamic

**Strong:**
- connection was the highlight
- relationship felt through off-stage focus
- excellent reactive listening throughout

**Average:**
- genuine partnership in spots
- listen more in earlier scenes
- let your partner's lines land

**Needs Work:**
- two soloists sharing a stage
- relationship not felt through focus
- react to your partner's moments

---

### Blocking / Off-Stage Focus

**Strong:**
- impeccable off-stage focus
- specific, consistent focal points
- blocking told a second story

**Average:**
- focus mostly maintained
- slipped during the climax
- drill the intense moments

**Needs Work:**
- looked directly at each other
- missing off-stage focal points
- hold specific spots per character

---

### Balance

**Strong:**
- beautifully balanced performance
- neither partner overshadowed the other
- both had something to do

**Average:**
- mostly balanced overall
- one partner dominated early
- even out investment throughout

**Needs Work:**
- one partner carried the piece
- other mainly a reactor
- give both enough to do

---

### Overall Effect

**Strong:**
- genuinely excellent DUO
- two performers felt essential
- a performance I'll remember

**Average:**
- solid piece, strong individual moments
- lean into the partnership
- responsive moments were the best

**Needs Work:**
- technical elements in place
- missing genuine partnership
- find the real connection
