# Dramatic Interpretation (DI) — Judge Prep Guide

> **High School & Middle School** | Max 10 minutes (30-second grace period) | No props or costumes | Published work

## Event Overview

In Dramatic Interpretation, a student performs a cutting from a **published work** (play, novel, short story, screenplay, etc.) that explores the human experience through dramatic — often dark, emotional, or serious — themes. The student performs alone, voicing all characters and narrating.

**Dramatic arc structure expected:**
- **Teaser** (30–45 sec): A moment of peak emotion to hook the audience
- **Introduction** (20–30 sec): Written by student; names the work, author, and establishes context
- **Exposition**: Setting and character establishment
- **Inciting Incident**: Event that sets the story in motion
- **Rising Action**: Building tension through conflict
- **Climax**: Peak emotional moment
- **Falling Action / Resolution**: Aftermath and conclusion

**Key rules:**
- Work must be published (not student-written)
- No props, costumes, or set pieces
- Off-stage focus: characters look at spots in the air rather than directly at the audience
- Time limit: 10 minutes, with a 30-second grace period

---

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Literature Selection & Cutting Quality
**What to evaluate:** Is the source material appropriate for DI? Does the cutting have dramatic integrity — a clear story with emotional depth and a complete arc?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Published work with literary merit; cutting includes full dramatic arc; material explores genuine human complexity; not overdone or gimmicky |
| **Good** | Solid selection; minor cuts feel abrupt; arc mostly present |
| **Needs Work** | Material too light for DI; cutting lacks clear story; ending feels incomplete; overly melodramatic or shock-value choices |

---

### 2. Introduction
**What to evaluate:** Does the intro name the work and author? Does it establish context without over-explaining? Is it engaging and original?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Smoothly transitions from teaser; names work/author; creates emotional stakes; does not spoil the story |
| **Good** | All required info present; intro functional but formulaic |
| **Needs Work** | Missing author or title; intro over-explains the plot; disconnected from teaser or body |

---

### 3. Characterization
**What to evaluate:** Can you tell characters apart? Are they consistent? Do they have internal logic (motivation, physicality, voice)?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Each character has a distinct, motivated voice and body; transitions between characters are clean and clear; characters have depth — not caricatures |
| **Good** | Characters are distinguishable; some consistency issues; vocal variety present but limited physical differentiation |
| **Needs Work** | Characters blend together; transitions are unclear; character choices feel arbitrary or stereotypical |

---

### 4. Blocking / Physical Performance
**What to evaluate:** Use of the performance space, physical differentiation between characters, focal points (off-stage eye focus), and use of the body to tell the story.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Space used meaningfully; off-stage focus is specific and consistent per character; physical choices reinforce emotional content; movement feels motivated |
| **Good** | Some spatial awareness; focal points mostly consistent; movement present but occasionally unmotivated |
| **Needs Work** | Performer stays in one spot; looks directly at audience during character dialogue; random or nervous movement |

---

### 5. Vocal Performance & Emotional Range
**What to evaluate:** Vocal variety, emotional authenticity, use of pace and pause, ability to convey the full spectrum of emotion the piece demands.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Wide, natural emotional range; voice carries genuine feeling; pacing honors both intensity and quiet; emotional shifts feel lived-in |
| **Good** | Emotional moments present; voice conveys feeling; some stretches feel performed rather than felt |
| **Needs Work** | Monotone or emotionally flat; emotions telegraphed without underlying truth; tension or pathos not conveyed |

---

### 6. Overall Effect / Impact
**What to evaluate:** Did the performance achieve what DI is meant to do — illuminate the human condition and create an emotional experience?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Genuinely moving; the audience understands and feels the story; performance lingered |
| **Good** | Story was clear and emotionally present; solid but didn't fully break through |
| **Needs Work** | Flat or confusing; audience may not have grasped the story's meaning or felt its weight |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **Off-stage focus:** Characters should look at specific spots in the air (not at the audience or the judge). If a performer breaks focus and looks directly at you during dialogue, note it.
- **Is this actually dramatic?** If it's funny, it may belong in HI. DI should explore genuine emotional depth — grief, loss, trauma, identity, moral conflict.
- **Published source:** The cutting must come from a published work. If you recognize it as student-written or cannot find a source, make a note (though verification is usually done by tab).
- **Transition clarity:** Can you tell when one character ends and another begins? Unclear transitions are a common weakness.
- **Narrator vs. character:** Does the student handle the shift between narration and characterization smoothly?
- **Emotional authenticity vs. performance:** Watch for "performing sadness" rather than actually conveying it. The best DI feels real.
- **Time violations:** There is a 30-second grace period; going past 10:30 is a rules violation — note it on the ballot (the tab handles consequences).

## Sample Judge Comments

### Literature Selection & Cutting

**Strong:**
- inspired selection with literary weight
- emotionally complex characters
- precise inciting-incident-to-climax arc

**Average:**
- solid source material
- cutting skips rising action
- climax feels sudden

**Needs Work:**
- leans comedic, not dramatic
- unintended laughter from audience
- choose higher dramatic stakes
- more emotional complexity

---

### Introduction

**Strong:**
- sets emotional tone immediately
- seamless teaser-to-intro transition
- made me care early

**Average:**
- names work and author
- gives context but just facts
- let intro feel something

**Needs Work:**
- author or work unnamed
- required info missing
- intro spoiled the ending
- preserve some mystery

---

### Characterization

**Strong:**
- vividly realized characters
- clear contrast between roles
- internally consistent with motivation

**Average:**
- main character well-developed
- secondary characters need specificity
- add distinct vocal or physical habit

**Needs Work:**
- characters hard to distinguish
- muddled dialogue-heavy scenes
- give each a pitch and tempo
- add a physical anchor

---

### Blocking / Physical Performance

**Strong:**
- thoughtful, deliberate use of space
- clear focal point per character
- staging reflects emotional geography

**Average:**
- focal points mostly consistent
- a few unintentional shifts
- use depth and levels more

**Needs Work:**
- looks directly at audience
- breaks off-stage focus convention
- set eye-level spots per character
- commit to them consistently

---

### Vocal Performance & Emotional Range

**Strong:**
- extraordinary emotional range
- heartbreak to anger authentically
- devastating silence before climax

**Average:**
- strong commitment in climax
- earlier scenes feel flatter
- let tension simmer underneath

**Needs Work:**
- narrow emotional register
- needs a full emotional journey
- push further, text supports it
- trust the material, commit fully

---

### Overall Effect

**Strong:**
- genuinely moving performance
- clear story, real characters
- lingered after it ended

**Average:**
- solid DI with real strengths
- story turns need more commitment
- trust the skills you have

**Needs Work:**
- technically sound foundation
- let the material affect you
- find a personal connection
- let it drive everything

---

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] Teaser before introduction
- [ ] Introduction names work and author
- [ ] Clear dramatic arc (exposition → climax → resolution)
- [ ] Distinct, motivated characters
- [ ] Off-stage focal points consistent per character
- [ ] Emotional authenticity (not just performed emotion)
- [ ] Vocal variety and use of pause
- [ ] Smooth transitions between characters
- [ ] Within 10-minute time limit
