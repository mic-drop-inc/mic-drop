# Original Oratory — Judge Prep Guide

> **High School Event** | Max 10 minutes (30-second grace period) | Memorized | Self-written | No props or costumes

## Event Overview

In Original Oratory (OO), students deliver a **self-written, ten-minute speech** on a topic of their choosing. The speech may be **informative or persuasive**, but in either case the orator builds an argument using **evidence, logic, and emotional appeals** — it is not an essay read aloud, but a researched, organized, and crafted call to think, feel, believe, or act. The speech is **delivered entirely from memory** and is the student's own original writing.

**Structure:**
- **Introduction** (~1–2 min): Engages the audience, establishes the significance of the topic, transitions to a clear thesis statement, and previews the major points
- **Body** (~6–8 min): 2–4 major arguments, each built on a **claim** (the point), a **warrant** (why it's valid), and an **impact** (why it matters / who it affects), supported by cited evidence
- **Conclusion** (~1 min): Restates the thesis, reviews the major points, and offers final thoughts that engage the audience and call them to action

**Key rules to know:**
- The speech must be **written by the student** (original) and **delivered from memory**
- **Only 150 directly quoted words** may be used; all source material — quoted or paraphrased — must be **orally cited**
- Topics may be informative or persuasive, value-oriented or policy-oriented
- No props or costumes
- Time limit: 10 minutes with a 30-second grace period

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Topic & Thesis
**What to evaluate:** Is the topic significant and relatable to the audience? Is there a clear, meaningful, arguable thesis that the rest of the speech sets out to prove or illuminate?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Topic is significant, timely, and relatable; thesis is clear, focused, and arrives early; everything in the speech serves it |
| **Good** | Worthwhile topic; thesis present but slightly broad, buried, or generic; mostly stays on point |
| **Needs Work** | Topic feels trivial or unfocused; thesis is unclear, missing, or so vague the speech wanders |

---

### 2. Originality & Content
**What to evaluate:** Is the argument the student's own, built from genuine research? Does it balance evidence, logic, and emotional appeal? Are sources cited orally, and does it stay within the quotation limit rather than leaning on others' words?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Fresh, original take; strong mix of evidence, logic, and emotional appeal; sources orally cited; clearly the student's own voice and writing |
| **Good** | Solid content with credible support; leans on one appeal more than others; citations present but occasionally thin or inconsistent |
| **Needs Work** | Feels like an essay or list of facts; little original insight; sources uncited; appears to rely on direct quotation rather than the student's own words |

---

### 3. Organization & Structure
**What to evaluate:** Does the speech move logically from intro to body to conclusion? Are the 2–4 main points distinct, well-ordered, and each built on claim, warrant, and impact?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Clear intro/body/conclusion; points ordered to build on one another; smooth transitions; each argument has a claim, warrant, and impact |
| **Good** | Recognizable structure; transitions a bit mechanical; one point may feel out of order or underdeveloped |
| **Needs Work** | Hard to tell where one point ends and the next begins; missing preview or review; arguments asserted without warrant or impact |

---

### 4. Vocal Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Rate, pitch, volume, articulation, and use of pause. Does the voice sound conversational yet polished, and does it serve the message?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Dynamic range; conversational and smooth; meaningful pauses; pacing and emphasis match the content's emotional and logical beats |
| **Good** | Clear and mostly varied; some stretches of consistent pace or volume; occasional rushing |
| **Needs Work** | Monotone, mumbled, or too fast/slow; sounds memorized or recited; hard to follow |

---

### 5. Physical Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Eye contact, gestures, posture, facial expression, and movement. Does the body reinforce the message without distracting from it?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Natural, purposeful movement; strong, distributed eye contact; gestures match words; expressive face; poised and controlled |
| **Good** | Good posture and eye contact; some intentional gestures but occasional rigidity or nervous habits |
| **Needs Work** | Minimal eye contact, distracting habits (swaying, fidgeting, pacing); gestures disconnected from meaning |

---

### 6. Connection / Authenticity & Persuasiveness
**What to evaluate:** Does the student own the message and speak directly to the audience? Is the speech persuasive — does it make you think, feel, or want to act?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Speaks directly to the audience; genuine investment in the topic; persuasive and moving; you leave thinking or feeling differently |
| **Good** | Mostly connected and believable; persuasive in places but occasionally feels rehearsed or detached |
| **Needs Work** | Feels like recitation; little visible investment; the argument doesn't land or move the audience |

---

### 7. Overall Effect / Impact
**What to evaluate:** Did the speech work as a whole? Did content and delivery come together to leave a clear, lasting impression?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Memorable and cohesive; message is clear and resonant; content and delivery reinforce each other; stays with you |
| **Good** | Effective overall; message communicated; one or two elements hold it back from being exceptional |
| **Needs Work** | Flat or forgettable; unclear takeaway; content and delivery don't yet support each other |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **Is this the student's own writing?** OO is original by definition. If passages sound lifted or over-reliant on quotation, note it.
- **Quotation limit:** Only 150 directly quoted words are permitted. You won't count them, but flag a speech that leans heavily on others' words instead of the student's own.
- **Oral citations:** Listen for sources being credited aloud. Strong orators cite their evidence; missing citations are worth a comment.
- **Thesis clarity:** Can you state the speech's main point in one sentence? If not, the thesis may be unclear.
- **Claim / warrant / impact:** Strong arguments don't just assert — they explain why the claim holds and why it matters.
- **Time violations:** The limit is 10 minutes with a 30-second grace period. Note overtime on the ballot.
- **Memorization lapses:** Minor stumbles are common and don't need comment unless they significantly disrupt the speech.
- **Essay vs. oration:** An oration is performed and argued, not merely read aloud. Flag delivery that sounds like reading an essay.

## Sample Judge Comments

### Topic & Thesis

**Strong:**
- topic clearly matters to you
- thesis arrived early and clear
- every point built toward it

**Average:**
- worthwhile topic
- thesis slow to come into focus
- state central claim earlier and directly

**Needs Work:**
- main point hard to identify
- several ideas, none committed to
- pick one specific thesis
- let every section serve it

---

### Originality & Content

**Strong:**
- clearly your argument, your words
- evidence, logic, and genuine emotion
- sources cited cleanly throughout
- original voice, the heart of the event

**Average:**
- solid content, credible sources
- leans heavily on facts
- add emotional appeal for stakes
- back more claims with citations

**Needs Work:**
- feels like an essay, not an oration
- facts listed, no argument built
- tell me what you think and why
- cite evidence aloud, your own words

---

### Organization & Structure

**Strong:**
- intro previewed where we were going
- points built logically on each other
- clear claim, warrant, and impact
- conclusion tied it together

**Average:**
- overall shape clear
- transitions felt mechanical
- one point seemed out of order
- order points by what audience needs first

**Needs Work:**
- hard to tell where points ended
- give each point claim, warrant, impact
- signpost your transitions
- make the structure followable

---

### Vocal Delivery

**Strong:**
- conversational, smooth, yet polished
- pace and volume matched the beats
- pause let the idea land

**Average:**
- clear articulation
- pace stayed fairly even
- slow down on strongest points
- let key lines breathe

**Needs Work:**
- sounded memorized, not spoken
- key moments rushed
- talk to the audience, not recite
- write in intentional pauses

---

### Physical Delivery

**Strong:**
- excellent, distributed eye contact
- felt like direct conversation
- gestures grew naturally from words

**Average:**
- good posture and eye contact
- gaze dropped during transitions
- make gestures more intentional
- let words drive the movement

**Needs Work:**
- swaying and fidgeting distracted
- find a grounded stance
- distribute eye contact across room
- match gestures to your words

---

### Connection / Authenticity & Persuasiveness

**Strong:**
- clear belief in the message
- spoke directly to us
- genuine investment in the topic
- left me thinking differently

**Average:**
- connection shone in the closing
- bring that energy earlier
- engage the audience throughout
- fully persuasive with more directness

**Needs Work:**
- felt like recitation, not conversation
- argument didn't quite move me
- decide what you want us to feel
- let that purpose drive every line

---

### Overall Effect

**Strong:**
- memorable oration
- meaningful topic in your own voice
- strong evidence and emotion
- vocal and physical control throughout

**Average:**
- solid speech with clear strengths
- find the moments that must land
- tighten the structure
- add emotional weight

**Needs Work:**
- foundation is here
- make it a living argument
- clarify thesis, cite evidence
- connect so they leave feeling something

---

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] Topic is significant and relatable; thesis is clear and early
- [ ] Speech is the student's own original writing
- [ ] Balances evidence, logic, and emotional appeal
- [ ] Sources cited orally; not over-reliant on direct quotation (150-word limit)
- [ ] Clear intro / body / conclusion with 2–4 main points
- [ ] Each argument has a claim, warrant, and impact
- [ ] Vocal variety (rate, pitch, volume, pause); conversational delivery
- [ ] Strong eye contact and purposeful physical presence
- [ ] Authentic, persuasive connection with the audience
- [ ] Delivered from memory, within 10 minutes (+30-sec grace)
