# Poetry Interpretation — Judge Prep Guide

> **Interpretation Event** | Max 5 minutes (including introduction) | Manuscript/binder required | Published poetry only — no prose, no drama

## Event Overview

In Poetry Interpretation, students perform an interpretation of **published poetry** — either a single long-form poem or a **program of poems** arranged around a theme or idea. The poems may come from one source or several. Poetry conveys ideas, experiences, and emotions through compressed, deliberate language; its focus on language and form is often designed to elicit critical thought, reflection, or emotion rather than simply to tell a story. The performance is delivered **with a manuscript** — typically a small three-ring binder with page protectors — and the student's handling of that binder ("bookwork") is part of the performance and is evaluated. This is **not** prose: reward sensitivity to verse, imagery, rhythm, line breaks, and the compression of poetic language.

**Structure:**
- **Teaser** (optional, ~0:00–0:30): Previews topic, theme, and mood through brief poem excerpts, before the intro
- **Introduction** (~0:30–1:00): In the student's own words, names the **titles and authors** and frames the theme/idea; must be memorized
- **Exposition** (~1:00–2:30): Develops the pieces, themes, and ideas, moving between poems
- **Build to Climax** (~3:30–4:30): Changes pace, tone, and volume to reach the emotional peak
- **Resolution** (~4:30–5:00): Pulls back in tone and concludes the major themes

**Key rules to know:**
- Material must be **published poetry** — traditional (formal meter or rhyme) or nontraditional (rhythmic flow without formal meter, e.g., spoken word / slam)
- **No prose and no drama (plays)** may be used in this category
- A **manuscript is required** — a binder/manuscript must be used; reading from a book or magazine is prohibited
- A program may draw poems from **one or multiple sources**
- The **introduction must be memorized** and include titles and authors; the rest need not be memorized, though strong performers master the script
- Time limit: **5 minutes including the introduction**; the teaser is optional and not required
- Movement (blocking) may be restricted by specific tournament rules — judges should note the local rules in effect

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Selection & Literary Merit
**What to evaluate:** Is this genuine, published poetry of real literary quality? Is it appropriate for the student's age, maturity, and range? Does the poetry reward interpretation (rather than simply being read on the page)?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Strong, published poetry with rich language and imagery; clearly suited to the performer; the poems gain something in performance |
| **Good** | Solid, legitimate poetry; mostly a good fit; some passages work better aloud than others |
| **Needs Work** | Thin or borderline material; poetry that resists interpretation; selection mismatched to the performer's age or range — or material that strays toward prose/drama |

---

### 2. Theme / Program Cohesion
**What to evaluate:** For a program of poems, do the pieces connect around a clear theme or idea? Is the arrangement purposeful? For a single poem, is its through-line clear?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Poems are arranged with clear intent; each piece earns its place; the theme builds and deepens across the program |
| **Good** | A theme is present; arrangement mostly works; one or two pieces feel loosely connected |
| **Needs Work** | Poems feel like a list rather than a program; no clear theme; arrangement seems arbitrary |

---

### 3. Interpretation & Meaning
**What to evaluate:** Does the student understand the poetry — its subtext, imagery, and compressed meaning? Do they reveal layers a casual reader would miss? Is the verse honored (not flattened into prose)?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Imagery and subtext come alive; the student illuminates meaning; the compression of poetic language is honored and made clear |
| **Good** | Surface meaning is clear; some images land; deeper subtext only partially explored |
| **Needs Work** | Words are spoken without evident understanding; imagery passes by unmarked; poetry is delivered as flat prose |

---

### 4. Use of the Binder / Manuscript (Bookwork)
**What to evaluate:** Is the required binder handled with purpose and control? Are page turns clean and motivated? Does the bookwork support the performance rather than distract from it?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Binder held comfortably; page turns are clean, unhurried, and motivated (marking a shift in poem, tone, or moment); bookwork enhances meaning |
| **Good** | Bookwork is competent; page turns are functional if occasionally mechanical; binder rarely distracts |
| **Needs Work** | Binder is gripped awkwardly or used as a barrier; rushed or jarring page turns; the manuscript pulls focus from the poetry |

---

### 5. Vocal Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Rate, pitch, volume, articulation, and pause. Does the voice honor the rhythm of the verse, the line breaks, and the music of the language without falling into a sing-song "beat"?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Vocal variety serves the verse; rhythm and line breaks are honored; rhyme and repetition are shaped, not hammered; pauses give images room to land |
| **Good** | Mostly clear and varied; rhythm is present; occasional rushing, monotone, or predictable hitting of the rhyme |
| **Needs Work** | Sing-song or monotone delivery; line breaks ignored or over-stressed; rushed pace flattens the language |

---

### 6. Physical Delivery & Focal Points
**What to evaluate:** Facial expression, gesture, posture, eye contact, and any motivated movement. Are focal points (where the student directs scenes or addresses) clear and consistent? Does the body reinforce the poetry?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Expression and gesture arise from the text; focal points are clear and consistent; movement (where allowed) is motivated and never gratuitous |
| **Good** | Generally controlled presence; some gestures and expression; focal points mostly consistent with minor drift |
| **Needs Work** | Stiff or distracting body; little facial life; unclear or wandering focal points; movement unmotivated by the text |

---

### 7. Introduction
**What to evaluate:** Does the memorized intro name the **titles and authors**, frame the theme in the student's own words, and draw the audience in before the body begins?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Titles and authors named naturally; theme framed compellingly; audience understands the lens before the poetry begins |
| **Good** | Required information present but intro feels formulaic or rushed; theme stated but not vivid |
| **Needs Work** | Missing titles or authors; theme unclear; intro disconnected from the poems that follow |

---

### 8. Overall Effect / Impact
**What to evaluate:** Did the program move you? Did the poetry — its images, rhythm, and meaning — stay with you afterward?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Memorable and resonant; the poetry landed emotionally; a clear impression lingered |
| **Good** | Solid and communicated; some moments connected; impact uneven |
| **Needs Work** | Flat or forgettable; the poetry never quite reached the audience |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **Is it actually poetry?** No prose and no drama (plays) are permitted. If the material reads as a monologue or short story, flag it.
- **Is the binder used?** The manuscript is required. Reading from a book or magazine is prohibited; a performance without the binder is a rules concern.
- **Published material:** Poetry should be published work, with titles and authors named in the intro.
- **The sing-song trap:** Watch for students who hit every rhyme hard or fall into a predictable beat. Reward those who vary repetition and let rhythm serve meaning.
- **Poetry is not prose:** Reward sensitivity to imagery, line breaks, and compressed language. Don't penalize a piece for being non-linear or ethereal — that can be the point.
- **Bookwork pace:** Rushed, jarring page turns are distracting. Smooth, motivated bookwork is a strength, even when basic.
- **Time:** The limit is 5 minutes including the introduction. Note significant overages on the ballot per the tournament's rules.
- **Local movement rules:** Some tournaments restrict blocking/movement. Judge within the rules in effect for your round.

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] Material is published poetry — no prose, no drama
- [ ] Manuscript/binder used (not a book or magazine)
- [ ] Single poem or a coherent themed program
- [ ] Memorized introduction names titles and authors
- [ ] Clear theme / through-line across the program
- [ ] Interpretation reveals imagery and subtext (not flat prose)
- [ ] Clean, motivated bookwork (page turns)
- [ ] Vocal variety honors rhythm and line breaks (no sing-song)
- [ ] Clear, consistent focal points and motivated physicality
- [ ] Within the 5-minute limit (including introduction)
