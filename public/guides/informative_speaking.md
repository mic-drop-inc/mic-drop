# Informative Speaking — Judge Prep Guide

> **High School** | Max 10 minutes | Self-written | Delivered from memory | Goal: Educate, NOT persuade | Max 150 directly quoted words | Oral citations required

## Event Overview

Informative Speaking is a prepared, self-written 10-minute speech designed to **educate** the audience on a topic. The student's job is to make the audience smarter about something — not to change their opinions or advocate for a position. The line between informative and persuasive is fundamental to this event.

**Key rule:** This is NOT Original Oratory. If the speech argues for a policy change, promotes a value judgment, or ends with a call to action, it belongs in OO, not Informative.

**Maximum 150 directly quoted words** from other sources. Beyond that, all language must be the student's own words. Oral citations are required for any quoted or cited material.

The speech is **delivered from memory** — no notes or manuscript.

**Speech Structure:**
- **Introduction**: Hook → Thesis → Preview of main points
- **Body**: 2–4 main points, each following: Claim → Warrant (evidence + citation) → Impact (why it matters)
- **Conclusion**: Restate thesis → Review main points → Memorable clincher

**Traits of strong Informative speakers:**
- Driven and deeply engaged with the topic
- Well-spoken and articulate
- Enthusiastic — genuine passion for sharing knowledge
- Logical and organized
- Personable and approachable

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Topic Selection & Appropriateness
**What to evaluate:** Is this a genuinely informative topic? Is it fresh, specific, and appropriate? Would the audience actually learn something meaningful?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Topic is educational and surprising; audience learns something substantive and applicable; topic isn't over-exposed; scope is manageable in 10 minutes |
| **Good** | Solid topic choice; somewhat familiar but handled with depth |
| **Needs Work** | Topic is either too broad (generic overview) or clearly persuasive (arguing for something); familiar topic presented superficially |

---

### 2. Content / Research Quality
**What to evaluate:** Are claims supported by credible, cited sources? Is the information accurate, specific, and substantive? Does the student demonstrate genuine expertise?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Multiple credible, cited sources; evidence is specific and recent; student explains the significance of evidence (not just lists facts); clearly has deep knowledge |
| **Good** | Evidence present with citations; solid research; could go deeper in places |
| **Needs Work** | Thin or uncited evidence; vague claims ("experts say…" without attribution); information feels like a Wikipedia-level survey |

---

### 3. Organization & Structure
**What to evaluate:** Is the speech clearly structured with a strong intro, organized body, and satisfying conclusion? Are transitions clear?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Clear thesis and preview; body points follow Claim-Warrant-Impact; smooth transitions; conclusion adds meaning, not just recap |
| **Good** | Structure present; transitions functional; conclusion is adequate |
| **Needs Work** | Hard to identify structure; points blend together; no clear thesis; conclusion is abrupt or missing |

---

### 4. Language & Word Choice
**What to evaluate:** Is the speech written with care? Does the language clarify or obscure? Is there evidence of craft in the writing itself?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Precise, vivid language; effective use of analogy and imagery to explain complex ideas; no jargon without explanation; evidence of a distinct voice |
| **Good** | Generally clear; some effective language choices; occasional vagueness |
| **Needs Work** | Vague or overly technical; unclear explanations; no distinctive voice; sounds like paraphrased research |

---

### 5. Vocal Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Is the speech engaging to listen to? Rate, volume, pitch, pause, emphasis.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Dynamic; emphasizes key ideas naturally; pace varies to serve meaning; pauses create emphasis; voice shows genuine enthusiasm |
| **Good** | Clear and audible; some vocal variety; generally engaging |
| **Needs Work** | Monotone or too fast; hard to absorb information; voice doesn't convey enthusiasm |

---

### 6. Physical Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Eye contact, gestures, movement, facial expression. Does the physical presence support the informative goal?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Strong eye contact distributed throughout room; natural, reinforcing gestures; physical presence conveys confidence and authority on the topic |
| **Good** | Generally good eye contact; decent gestures; minor nervous habits |
| **Needs Work** | Limited eye contact; rigid or nervous physical presence; movement is distracting |

---

### 7. Informative vs. Persuasive Distinction
**What to evaluate:** Is the speech genuinely informative, or does it drift into advocacy and persuasion?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Clearly stays in educational territory; presents multiple perspectives where relevant; does not argue for a position or end with a call to action |
| **Good** | Mostly informative; may have slight persuasive lean but doesn't cross into OO territory |
| **Needs Work** | Speech is essentially an OO — makes a clear value argument, advocates for change, or ends with "we must…" style call to action |

---

### 8. Overall Educational Impact
**What to evaluate:** Did the audience actually learn something? Was it interesting?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Audience leaves with clear, organized knowledge they didn't have before; speech was engaging and memorable; student's passion for the topic was infectious |
| **Good** | Educational value is present; some engagement; some moments of real illumination |
| **Needs Work** | Forgettable; information didn't land; felt like a report read aloud |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **The informative/persuasive line:** This is the most important distinction. If the speech argues that "we should do X" or "X is bad and must change," it's OO content in an Informative round. Note this explicitly.
- **150-word quotation limit:** You can't count precisely, but if the speech seems to consist substantially of read quotations, note concern.
- **Oral citations:** Every claim from an external source must be attributed. "According to Dr. Jane Smith of Johns Hopkins in 2023…" is the model. Vague attributions weaken credibility.
- **Visual aids:** Informative is one of the events where visual aids *may* be permitted depending on the tournament. Check your tab instructions. At some Nationals tournaments they are permitted; at others, not.
- **Self-written requirement:** The speech must be written by the student, not a coach or AI. If the language seems implausibly polished or doesn't match the student's delivery, note it.
- **Time limit:** 10 minutes maximum. Note any violation.

## Sample Judge Comments

### Topic Selection

**Strong:**
- genuinely fresh, unfamiliar topic
- specific angle, not broad survey
- clear personal fascination

**Average:**
- solid, substantive topic
- find a sharper, narrower angle
- surface the surprising aspect

**Needs Work:**
- drifts into persuasive territory
- arguing a position, not explaining
- reframe to stay educational
- consider whether this belongs in OO

---

### Content / Research Quality

**Strong:**
- specific, credible sources throughout
- current, directly relevant evidence
- beyond surface knowledge

**Average:**
- solid, accurate content
- name study, author, and year

**Needs Work:**
- unsupported or vague claims
- "research shows" with no attribution
- name your sources

---

### Organization & Structure

**Strong:**
- clear thesis and preview
- consistent Claim-Warrant-Impact
- conclusion adds a dimension

**Average:**
- structure present, transitions abrupt
- add bridging sentences between points

**Needs Work:**
- hard to track structure
- add a preview in the intro
- clear signpost transitions needed

---

### Language & Word Choice

**Strong:**
- analogy makes concept accessible
- clear, distinct voice
- authored, not assembled

**Average:**
- clear, appropriate language
- add vivid, specific imagery

**Needs Work:**
- unexplained technical terms
- explain everything for the audience
- replace vague phrasing with specifics

---

### Vocal Delivery

**Strong:**
- emphasizes key terms well
- pauses let information land
- genuine enthusiasm in voice

**Average:**
- clear and audible throughout
- slow down on key facts
- vary pace to signal importance

**Needs Work:**
- consistent pace, hard to absorb
- mark and slow key points
- vary rate and volume

---

### Physical Delivery

**Strong:**
- confident posture
- purposeful, illustrative gestures
- eye contact reaches whole room

**Average:**
- steady presence, good eye contact
- let gestures do descriptive work

**Needs Work:**
- eye contact drops on dense passages
- limited gestures, feels read
- ground stance, distribute eye contact

---

### Informative vs. Persuasive

**Strong:**
- holds the informative frame
- never argues a position
- lets information speak for itself

**Average:**
- mostly informative
- watch evaluative words ("unfortunately", "should")
- swap for neutral phrasing

**Needs Work:**
- shifts to persuasive by conclusion
- "we must / we should" language
- educate, don't advocate
- let audience draw conclusions

---

### Overall Effect

**Strong:**
- substantive and well-organized
- beautifully written
- delivered with genuine passion

**Average:**
- solid speech with real strengths
- let enthusiasm show more in delivery

**Needs Work:**
- researched material, clear topic
- organize more clearly
- back each claim with a citation

---

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] Topic is informative, NOT persuasive (no "we should/must" calls to action)
- [ ] Self-written by student
- [ ] Clear thesis and preview in introduction
- [ ] Body follows Claim-Warrant-Impact structure
- [ ] Oral citations for all external evidence
- [ ] Direct quotation within 150-word limit
- [ ] Smooth transitions between points
- [ ] Conclusion adds meaning (not just recap)
- [ ] Vocal variety and engagement
- [ ] Strong eye contact and physical presence
- [ ] Within 10-minute time limit
