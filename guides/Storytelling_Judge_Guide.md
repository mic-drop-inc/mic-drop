# Storytelling — Judge Prep Guide

> **Interpretation Event** (middle & high school) | Max 5 minutes (includes intro) | Memorized — no manuscript | No props (a chair is permitted)

## Event Overview

In Storytelling, students select a **published story** and perform it for an audience **as if telling it to a group of young children**. The performer is the warm, animated teller drawing the listeners into the world of the story — direct, expressive, and audience-aware. The whole performance is memorized; there is no binder or manuscript.

Some tournaments specify a **theme or genre** for the season (e.g., fairy tales, heroism, mystery, Americana, Native American tales, campfire stories), and the chosen story must fit that theme. **The National Tournament does NOT require a theme** — when no theme is prescribed, any appropriate selection is fair game, and an off-theme piece should not be penalized.

**Structure:** (times are approximate)
- **Teaser** (0:00–0:30): Previews the story, characters, and style. *Optional — not required.*
- **Introduction** (0:30–1:00): The student, in their own words, introduces the piece. Must be memorized and include the **title and author**.
- **Exposition & Inciting Incident** (1:00–2:30): Establishes character, setting, and theme; sends the conflict into motion.
- **Rising Action & Climax** (2:30–4:15): Complicates the conflict; builds to the emotional peak.
- **Falling Action & Resolution** (4:15–5:00): Resolves the conflict and concludes the story.

**Key rules to know:**
- Story must be **published** and appropriate for young children; told as if presenting to that audience.
- **Memorized** — manuscripts are not permitted.
- Time limit: **no more than 5 minutes**, and that includes the introduction.
- **No props or costumes.** A **chair is permitted** and may be used creatively (sit to chat with the "children," stand on it briefly for effect, hide behind it as a wall) — but it must **not** be used as a prop.
- Performers may use a full range of movement and the whole stage space, or may choose to stay more contained.
- If a theme is specified by the tournament, the story must fit it. At a no-theme tournament (including Nationals), any appropriate story is allowed.

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Selection & Fit
**What to evaluate:** Is the story a strong, age-appropriate published piece with enough plot and character to entertain in five minutes? If the tournament has a theme, does the piece fit it? *(Do not penalize an off-theme piece if no theme was prescribed.)*

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Charming, well-suited story; rich plot and characters; clearly appropriate for young children; fits the theme (when one applies) |
| **Good** | Solid choice; plot or characters a bit thin, or theme fit is loose; mostly works for the age frame |
| **Needs Work** | Story too complex, too slight, or inappropriate for children; off-theme where a theme was required; hard to convey in five minutes |

---

### 2. Introduction
**What to evaluate:** Does the memorized intro, in the student's own words, name the **title and author** and invite us into the story? Does it set the tone?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Warm, inviting intro in the student's own words; title and author included naturally; sets up the story's tone and pulls us in |
| **Good** | Title and author present, but intro feels rote or rushed; modest setup |
| **Needs Work** | Missing title or author; intro disconnected from the story or read-like rather than told |

---

### 3. Narrative Clarity & Arc
**What to evaluate:** Is the story easy to follow from beginning to end? Does the cutting deliver a complete arc — setup, rising action, climax, resolution — within the time?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Clear, complete arc; conflict and stakes land; the ending satisfies; cutting feels whole, not chopped |
| **Good** | Mostly clear; a transition or plot point is fuzzy; arc largely complete |
| **Needs Work** | Hard to follow; jumps or gaps in the story; no clear climax or resolution; feels incomplete |

---

### 4. Audience Engagement & Direct Address
**What to evaluate:** Does the student tell the story *to* us as if we were children — warm, inviting, eye contact, drawing us in? Or does it feel like a recitation aimed at no one?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Genuinely connects with the audience; treats us like the children the story is for; eye contact and energy make us feel included |
| **Good** | Some connection and direct address; engagement dips during transitions or quieter sections |
| **Needs Work** | Performs at the wall or floor; little eye contact; we feel like observers, not the audience being told to |

---

### 5. Characterization & Voices
**What to evaluate:** Are the characters distinct and alive? Are voices, faces, and shifts clear enough that we always know who is "speaking"?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Vivid, distinct characters; voices and faces shift cleanly and consistently; even small characters feel real and fun |
| **Good** | Characters mostly distinct; a voice or two blurs together, or shifts are occasionally unclear |
| **Needs Work** | Characters sound and look alike; we lose track of who is speaking; flat or inconsistent portrayals |

---

### 6. Vocal Delivery & Animation
**What to evaluate:** Pace, pitch, volume, diction, pause, and inflection. Does the voice bring energy, color, and clarity to the telling?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Lively, expressive range; pacing and pauses build suspense and fun; every word clear; voice carries the mood |
| **Good** | Generally clear and varied; some monotone stretches or rushed moments; energy occasionally dips |
| **Needs Work** | Flat, mumbled, or too fast/slow; little variety; hard to follow; energy doesn't match a story for children |

---

### 7. Physical Delivery, Movement & Blocking
**What to evaluate:** Are gestures, facial expression, posture, and movement motivated by the story? If a chair is used, is it used safely and within the rules (not as a prop)?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Purposeful, expressive movement that serves the text; alive facial expressions; any chair use is creative, safe, and rule-legal |
| **Good** | Appropriate gestures and movement; some moments feel unmotivated or stiff; chair used adequately if at all |
| **Needs Work** | Movement for its own sake or none at all; nervous habits; chair used unsafely or as a prop; expression flat |

---

### 8. Overall Effect, Charm & Impact
**What to evaluate:** Did the telling delight you? Were you carried into the story the way a child would be? Did it leave a clear impression?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Charming, captivating, and memorable; you were swept into the story; full commitment and joy throughout |
| **Good** | Enjoyable and competent; clear story; some moments of real charm amid steadier stretches |
| **Needs Work** | Felt flat, rushed, or unconvincing; little charm or connection; the story didn't fully land |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **The chair is allowed — props are not.** A performer may sit, stand on, or hide behind a chair creatively. Don't mis-flag legitimate chair use as a prop violation. *Do* note it if the chair is used as a prop (a literal stand-in object) or used unsafely.
- **Theme only applies if the tournament prescribed one.** At a no-theme tournament (including Nationals), do not penalize an off-theme selection. Check the tournament's invitation before judging selection on theme.
- **Is the story memorized?** Manuscripts are not permitted. Minor stumbles are normal; flag only major lapses that disrupt the telling.
- **Is the intro complete?** It must include the title and author, in the student's own words.
- **Time:** Maximum 5 minutes including the intro. Time it and note if the performance runs over.
- **Age-appropriateness:** The story is meant for young children — flag content that isn't suitable for that audience.
- **Direct address:** Is the student telling the story *to* the audience as children, or reciting into space? This is the defining lens of the event.

## Sample Judge Comments

### Selection & Fit

**Strong:**
- delightful, well-pitched choice
- enough plot and personality for five minutes
- talking characters offer rich material

**Average:**
- fun selection but thin middle
- not enough happening to build energy
- needs one more turn or surprise

**Needs Work:**
- leaned grown-up, not child-friendly
- "telling to children" frame hard to sustain
- try clearer, simpler arc
- characters a young audience would love

---

### Introduction

**Strong:**
- warm, inviting intro
- title and author in own words
- playful tone set before starting

**Average:**
- title and author present
- felt rushed and rote
- slow down, let it welcome

**Needs Work:**
- title or author not caught
- both are required
- name piece and author in own words
- ease us into the world

---

### Narrative Clarity & Arc

**Strong:**
- whole story told beautifully
- setup, climax, satisfying ending
- never felt lost

**Average:**
- mostly held together
- jump into climax too fast
- add bridging line or clearer beat

**Needs Work:**
- skipped between events, hard to follow
- unclear resolution
- map out key plot points
- carry us cleanly start to end

---

### Audience Engagement & Direct Address

**Strong:**
- truly told it *to* us
- room felt like a circle of children
- eye contact pulled us in

**Average:**
- connected in lively moments
- gaze drifted during transitions
- keep telling it *to* someone

**Needs Work:**
- performed more than told
- looked past the audience
- picture real children in front
- share story directly with them

---

### Characterization & Voices

**Strong:**
- every character a joy
- voices and faces shift cleanly
- side characters as alive as the hero

**Average:**
- main character vivid
- a few others blur vocally
- give each a small signature

**Needs Work:**
- characters sound and look alike
- lost track of who's talking
- pick clear, fun choices per character
- commit consistently throughout

---

### Vocal Delivery & Animation

**Strong:**
- voice carried the storytelling
- pacing built suspense
- crisp, clear diction

**Average:**
- clear diction, some variety
- pace and pitch flatten out
- lean into whispers, pauses, bursts

**Needs Work:**
- flat and a touch fast
- hard to follow, dimmed fun
- exaggerate the highs and lows
- be big, playful, goofy

---

### Physical Delivery, Movement & Blocking

**Strong:**
- movement always served the story
- could see the setting
- clever, safe chair use

**Average:**
- appropriate gestures and expressions
- a few unmotivated movements
- let blocking come from the text

**Needs Work:**
- physically stiff
- nervous habits crept in
- let face and body get bigger
- show the world, don't just say it

---

### Overall Effect, Charm & Impact

**Strong:**
- charming, captivating telling
- swept into the story
- full commitment, left smiling

**Average:**
- warm, competent, flashes of charm
- push committed energy throughout
- especially the quieter sections

**Needs Work:**
- memorized, knows the shape
- next step: fully play
- find the joy and goofiness
- let it carry every choice

---

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] Published story, appropriate for young children
- [ ] Fits the tournament theme (only if a theme was prescribed)
- [ ] Memorized — no manuscript
- [ ] Introduction (own words) includes title and author
- [ ] Complete narrative arc within the cutting
- [ ] Told *to* the audience as children (direct address, eye contact)
- [ ] Distinct, consistent characters and voices
- [ ] Lively, varied, clear vocal delivery
- [ ] Motivated movement; chair (if used) is safe and not a prop
- [ ] Within the 5-minute limit (includes intro)
