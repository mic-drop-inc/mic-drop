# Prose Interpretation — Judge Prep Guide

> **Interpretation Event** | Max 5 minutes (including introduction) | Published prose, fiction or non-fiction | Manuscript/binder required | NOT poetry or drama

## Event Overview

In Prose Interpretation, students perform an interpretation of **published prose** — a short story, an excerpt from a novel, an essay, an article, or a biography. Prose is the "other" interpretation category: it isn't poetry, and it isn't drama. It corresponds to the **usual, everyday patterns of speech** (in contrast to poetic verse and language) and may combine **narration and dialogue**. A single performance draws on **one selection from one piece of literature**, and the performer reads from a **manuscript held in a binder** — the use of that binder (bookwork) is part of the performance and is evaluated.

Unlike poetry — which rewards sensitivity to verse, rhythm, and condensed language — prose rewards **narrative storytelling**: building a story arc and giving each character and the narrator a distinct, believable voice.

**Structure (times are approximate; the whole cutting must fit within 5 minutes):**
- **Teaser** (0:00–0:30): Previews the topic and mood. Teasers are NOT required.
- **Introduction** (0:30–1:00): In the student's own words, names the **title and author** and frames the piece. Must be memorized.
- **Exposition & Inciting Incident** (1:00–2:30): Introduces characters and setting; sets the conflict in motion.
- **Rising Action & Climax** (2:30–4:30): Complicates the conflict; builds to the emotional peak.
- **Falling Action & Resolution** (4:30–5:00): Resolves the conflict and concludes the story.

**Key rules to know:**
- Material must be **published prose** — fiction or non-fiction (short stories, novels, essays, articles, biographies).
- **No poetry and no drama (plays)** may be used.
- A performance uses **a single piece of literature**. Even from an anthology or collection, **only one selection** may be performed.
- A **manuscript is required**, typically a small three-ring binder with page protectors. **Reading from a book or magazine is prohibited.**
- The introduction must be memorized and include the **title and author**.
- Time limit: **5 minutes including the introduction** (timekeeper typically signals as the limit approaches).

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Selection & Literary Merit
**What to evaluate:** Did the student choose a published prose piece with genuine literary value and a clear story to tell? Is it well-suited to the performer's age, maturity, and range? Does the cutting form a complete, self-contained arc within five minutes?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Rich, worthwhile prose; cutting forms a complete narrative arc; well-matched to the student's maturity and vocal/physical range; works as a standalone piece |
| **Good** | Solid published selection; cutting mostly complete; a few transitions feel abrupt or some material strains the performer |
| **Needs Work** | Thin or poorly suited material; cutting feels choppy or incomplete; piece beyond the student's range, or possibly poetry/drama rather than prose |

---

### 2. Narrative / Storytelling Arc
**What to evaluate:** Does the performance tell a story? Is there exposition, an inciting incident, rising action to a clear climax, and a resolution? Does the tension build and release with purpose?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Clear arc from setup to climax to resolution; tension builds deliberately; pacing serves the story; the climax lands |
| **Good** | Story is followable; arc present but the climax under-builds or the pacing is uneven in places |
| **Needs Work** | Hard to follow what happens; events feel like a list rather than a story; no discernible build or emotional peak |

---

### 3. Characterization & Narrator Voice
**What to evaluate:** Are characters distinct and believable? Is the narrator a defined voice rather than a flat reader? When characters speak to each other, can you tell who is who? Are any accents or dialects handled consistently and authentically?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Each character has a distinct, consistent voice and physicality; narrator voice is clearly defined; transitions between voices are clean; accents (if any) are authentic and sustained |
| **Good** | Characters mostly distinguishable; some voices blur or slip; narrator present but occasionally flat |
| **Needs Work** | Characters sound alike; can't tell who is speaking; narrator is a flat read; accents inconsistent or distracting |

---

### 4. Use of the Binder / Manuscript (Bookwork)
**What to evaluate:** Bookwork is required and evaluated. Is the binder held comfortably and naturally? Are page turns smooth and motivated (scene changes, tonal shifts, dramatic beats)? Does the binder ever serve as an imagined property? Is the bookwork an integrated part of the performance rather than a distraction?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Binder handled with ease; page turns are smooth and motivated by the text; bookwork enhances meaning (e.g., closing on the intro/conclusion, turning on tone shifts); never rushed |
| **Good** | Competent, basic bookwork; binder held steadily; page turns functional but not yet expressive |
| **Needs Work** | Binder is awkward or distracting; pages rushed, fumbled, or zipped through; bookwork pulls focus from the story; reliance on reading rather than performing |

---

### 5. Vocal Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Rate, pitch, tone, volume, diction, inflection, and pause. Does the voice carry the narration and distinguish dialogue? Are pauses used to shape meaning and emotion?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Dynamic range; intentional pauses; clear diction; vocal choices match narrative and emotional shifts; dialogue clearly delineated |
| **Good** | Mostly clear; some variety; occasional rushing, monotone stretches, or dropped volume |
| **Needs Work** | Monotone, mumbled, or too fast; pauses absent or arbitrary; voice doesn't carry the story or separate characters |

---

### 6. Physical Delivery & Use of Focal Points
**What to evaluate:** Gestures, facial expression, posture, and the use of **focal points** (offstage focus) for character interaction. When characters speak to each other, does the performer place them with consistent focal points rather than swinging the head back and forth? Is any movement (blocking) motivated by the text?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Focal points are consistent and meaningful (placement, eye level, status); expressions and gestures arise from the text; any blocking is motivated and clean |
| **Good** | Focal points mostly consistent; gestures appropriate but sometimes generic; expression present but not fully alive |
| **Needs Work** | Focal points absent or shifting; head-swinging during dialogue; rigid posture, nervous habits, or movement that doesn't match the text |

---

### 7. Introduction
**What to evaluate:** Does the memorized intro name the **title and author** and frame the piece in the student's own words? Does it set up the theme and mood without giving everything away?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Title and author named naturally; intro frames theme/mood and draws the audience in; smooth handoff into the piece |
| **Good** | Title and author present; intro functional but formulaic, rushed, or only loosely connected to the piece |
| **Needs Work** | Title or author missing; intro disconnected from the performance, or appears to be read rather than memorized |

---

### 8. Overall Effect / Impact
**What to evaluate:** Did the performance suspend time and make you care about the story? Did the elements — selection, arc, character, voice, bookwork — combine into a cohesive, moving whole?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Memorable and emotionally resonant; story and characters stayed with you; all elements served a unified vision |
| **Good** | Solid, engaging performance; story communicated; some emotional connection but moments fell flat |
| **Needs Work** | Performance felt flat or disjointed; unclear what the story was or why it mattered |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **Is it actually prose?** No poetry and no drama (plays) are permitted. If the selection reads as verse or a stage play, note it.
- **Is the binder used?** A manuscript/binder is required and reading from a book or magazine is prohibited. Bookwork is evaluated — don't treat the binder as a neutral prop.
- **One selection only:** A prose performance uses a single piece of literature; even from an anthology, only one selection may be performed.
- **Does the intro name title and author?** Both are required, and the intro should be memorized.
- **Time violations:** Over 5 minutes (intro included) is a rules issue; note it on the ballot.
- **Character distinction:** Can you tell characters apart by voice and focal point? This is the heart of prose interpretation.
- **Accent risk:** If the piece calls for an accent or dialect, is it consistent and authentic, or distracting?
- **Familiarity vs. memorization:** Memorization is not required in Prose, but the strongest performers know the script so well they can lift their eyes off the page and perform. Heavy reliance on reading is a flaw to note.

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] Material is published prose (no poetry, no drama)
- [ ] A single piece of literature / one selection only
- [ ] Manuscript/binder used (not reading from a book or magazine)
- [ ] Bookwork smooth and motivated, not rushed or distracting
- [ ] Introduction memorized; names title and author
- [ ] Clear narrative arc (exposition → climax → resolution)
- [ ] Distinct, consistent characters and a defined narrator voice
- [ ] Consistent focal points for character interaction (no head-swinging)
- [ ] Vocal variety (rate, pitch, tone, volume, pause)
- [ ] Within 5-minute time limit (introduction included)
