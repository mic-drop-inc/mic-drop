# Extemporaneous Speaking — Judge Prep Guide

> **Two events:** US Extemporaneous (USX) and International Extemporaneous (IX)
> 30 minutes prep | Max 7 minutes speech | No notes during speech | Internet allowed during prep at Nationals

## Event Overview

Extemporaneous Speaking tests a student's ability to quickly analyze a current events question and deliver a well-organized, evidence-based speech on the spot. Students draw **3 questions**, select **1**, and have **30 minutes** to prepare before speaking for up to **7 minutes** (no minimum, but shorter speeches are penalized in competition norms).

**USX (United States Extemp):** Focuses on domestic US current events, politics, economics, and social policy.

**IX (International Extemp):** Focuses on international affairs, foreign policy, global economics, and world events.

**Speech Structure:**
1. **Introduction**: Hook → Q&A (answer to the question upfront) → Thesis → Preview of main points
2. **Body**: 2–3 main points, each with:
   - Sub-claim
   - Evidence (with cited sources)
   - Explanation/impact
3. **Conclusion**: Restate Q&A → Review main points → Clincher

**Key rules at Nationals:**
- Students may use the internet during their 30-minute prep
- No notes during the speech — must be delivered from memory
- One notecard permitted at some tournaments (check specific tournament rules)
- Time limit: 7 minutes maximum

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Answering the Question / Analysis
**What to evaluate:** Does the speech actually answer the question drawn? Is there a clear thesis? Does the analysis go beyond surface knowledge to show genuine understanding?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Directly answers the question; clear, defensible thesis; analysis shows depth and nuance; aware of complexity/counterarguments |
| **Good** | Answers the question; thesis present; analysis is adequate but somewhat surface-level |
| **Needs Work** | Vague or avoided answer; no clear thesis; analysis is thin or generic; doesn't actually engage the question |

---

### 2. Organization & Structure
**What to evaluate:** Is the speech structured according to the Q&A format? Clear intro/preview, organized body, clean conclusion?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Seamless organization; Q&A answer stated upfront; preview sets up body clearly; transitions are smooth; conclusion mirrors intro |
| **Good** | Structure present; Q&A format used; minor structural gaps; transitions are functional |
| **Needs Work** | Hard to follow; thesis unclear or absent; no clear preview; body points feel disconnected; conclusion is abrupt or missing |

---

### 3. Evidence & Source Citation
**What to evaluate:** Are claims backed by specific, cited evidence? Are sources credible and current? Does the student attribute quotes and statistics?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Multiple credible, current sources cited by name; evidence is specific (not vague "experts say"); sources match the points they support |
| **Good** | Evidence present and cited; some sources are vague or not fully attributed; evidence supports points |
| **Needs Work** | Little to no citation; "studies show" without attribution; evidence is general knowledge only; or conversely, speech is just a string of facts without analysis |

---

### 4. Analysis & Argumentation
**What to evaluate:** Beyond evidence, can the student connect the dots? Do they explain *why* evidence matters, what it means, and what follows from it?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Evidence is clearly linked to thesis; student explains cause-effect, significance, and implications; original insight present |
| **Good** | Adequate linking of evidence to argument; some moments of genuine analysis |
| **Needs Work** | Speech is a list of facts without interpretation; "so what" is never answered; no original analysis |

---

### 5. Vocal & Physical Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Given that this is an off-the-cuff speech, assess delivery charitably — but clarity, confidence, and engagement still matter.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Confident and conversational; appropriate pace; eye contact throughout; gestures feel natural; voice is engaging |
| **Good** | Mostly clear; some filler words or nervous habits; eye contact generally maintained |
| **Needs Work** | Significant filler words; looking away frequently; monotone; pace too fast or halting |

---

### 6. Overall Effect / Persuasiveness
**What to evaluate:** Did the speech advance a clear position on the question? Did you find it intellectually engaging?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Left with a clear answer to the question; compelling argument; student's engagement with the topic was evident |
| **Good** | Position was clear; argument was solid; some engagement |
| **Needs Work** | Unclear position; didn't feel like the student grasped the significance of the question |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **Does the speech actually answer the question?** This is the most fundamental criterion. A perfectly delivered speech that doesn't answer the question should rank low.
- **The Q&A structure:** Extemp speeches should state the answer to the question (the thesis) early in the introduction — not at the end. If a student builds to the answer suspense-style, that's a structural weakness.
- **Source credibility and currency:** In USX, expect sources like NYT, WSJ, Washington Post, Pew Research, Congressional Budget Office, etc. In IX, expect Foreign Affairs, The Economist, Reuters, BBC, etc. Vague attributions like "scientists say" or "according to studies" are weak.
- **Filler words:** Common in extemp because it's under-prep. Note if excessive, but be charitable — this is limited prep.
- **Internet use at Nationals:** Students can use internet during prep. This means their evidence base should be stronger and more current than at regular tournaments.
- **Time:** 7-minute max. Note violations. Also, speeches under ~5 minutes are typically considered too short for competitive rankings (though rules vary — check tab).
- **Do not intervene during timing issues:** Signal timing if asked (some tournaments have a timer panel), but don't coach.

## Sample Judge Comments

### Answering the Question / Analysis

**Strong:** "You answered the question directly in your introduction, which is exactly right. Your thesis was clear and defensible, and more impressively, you acknowledged the complexity of the issue before arguing your position. That level of nuance is what separates strong extemp analysis from surface-level commentary."

**Average:** "Your speech addressed the general topic of the question, but the thesis took a long time to arrive. State your position clearly and early — judges and audiences need to know what you're arguing before you present your evidence."

**Needs Work:** "I was not sure what your answer to the question was, even after listening to the full speech. Extemp speeches have a specific structure: Q&A (restate the question and answer it directly), then thesis, then preview. That upfront answer is essential to everything that follows."

---

### Organization & Structure

**Strong:** "Your organization was excellent — the Q&A format was cleanly executed, your preview told me exactly what was coming, each body point was clearly delineated, and your conclusion circled back beautifully. I could follow your argument effortlessly."

**Average:** "The structure was mostly there, though the transitions between your main points were somewhat abrupt. A clean signpost transition — 'my second reason is...' or 'turning now to...' — helps the audience track where you are in the argument."

**Needs Work:** "The speech was difficult to follow because the structure wasn't clear. I couldn't identify your main points or where one ended and another began. Extemp has a very specific structure — intro with Q&A and preview, then 2-3 clearly-labeled body points, then a conclusion that restates your answer. Work on making that architecture visible."

---

### Evidence & Source Citation

**Strong:** "Your sourcing was impressive — you cited specific, credible, and current sources for each claim, and you attributed quotes accurately. Citing [source] for that statistic and [source] for the policy example gave your argument real weight."

**Average:** "You cited sources in the speech, which is great. To strengthen your evidence, be more specific in your attributions — 'according to a 2024 Pew Research Center survey' is stronger than 'according to Pew.' Specificity signals you actually read the source."

**Needs Work:** "The speech lacked oral citations. In extemporaneous speaking, every factual claim should be attributed — who said it, who found it, what organization published it, and ideally when. Without citations, claims feel like personal opinion rather than researched fact."

---

### Analysis & Argumentation

**Strong:** "What set this speech apart was the analysis — you didn't just tell me what happened, you told me why it matters, what it means for the future, and how it connects to the larger question. That's the difference between reporting and genuine extemp analysis."

**Average:** "The evidence you cited was strong, but in some places you moved on without explaining why it supports your thesis. After each piece of evidence, ask yourself: 'so what? why does this matter to my argument?' That explanation is the heart of good analysis."

**Needs Work:** "The speech felt more like a news report than an argument. You listed relevant facts and events, but I needed you to tell me what they mean and how they answer the question. Analysis is the bridge between evidence and thesis — build that bridge explicitly."

---

### Vocal & Physical Delivery

**Strong:** "For a limited-prep event, your delivery was remarkable — conversational, confident, and controlled. Your pace allowed ideas to land, your eye contact was consistent, and you never felt like you were retrieving memorized text. You sounded like you were thinking through the argument in real time."

**Average:** "Delivery was solid overall. Watch the filler words — 'um' and 'like' appeared frequently during transitions, which is common in limited prep but can be reduced with practice. Your eye contact was good, though you occasionally looked up-left when retrieving information."

**Needs Work:** "Delivery was the main challenge this round — a rapid pace made it hard to absorb the content, and frequent filler words ('um,' 'like,' 'so') made the speech feel uncertain. In extemp prep, consider practicing transitions aloud — those are where filler words tend to cluster."

---

### Overall Effect

**Strong:** "This was a genuinely compelling extemp speech — you answered the question, backed it up with current sourcing, and delivered it with confidence. I walked away with a clear answer and a new perspective on the issue."

**Average:** "A solid speech with a clear argument and good research. To get to the next level, push the analysis deeper — the most memorable extemp speeches make the audience think differently about an issue, not just learn facts about it."

**Needs Work:** "The foundation is there — you drew a question, chose it, and prepared a speech. Now focus on the depth of the argument. The question deserves more than a survey of the issue — it needs a clear answer defended by specific evidence and genuine analysis."

---

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] Question answered directly (not danced around)
- [ ] Q&A format used (answer stated early, not at end)
- [ ] Clear thesis stated in introduction
- [ ] Main points previewed in introduction
- [ ] Each body point has: claim + evidence + analysis
- [ ] Oral citations with source names (and dates where possible)
- [ ] Transitions between main points clear
- [ ] Conclusion restates answer and reviews points
- [ ] Delivered from memory (no notes during speech, unless notecard is permitted)
- [ ] Within 7-minute time limit
