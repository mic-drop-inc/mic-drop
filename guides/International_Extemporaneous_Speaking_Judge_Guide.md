# International Extemporaneous Speaking (IX) — Judge Prep Guide

> **High School Event** | 30 minutes prep | Max 7 minutes speech | Delivered from memory | Focus: International / foreign affairs

## Event Overview

In International Extemporaneous Speaking (IX), a student is presented with a **choice of three questions** related to current events outside the United States and selects **one**. With **30 minutes** to prepare, they build and deliver a **seven-minute speech** answering the chosen question. During prep, students may consult research (their "files") they have gathered beforehand, and at the National Tournament they may use the internet. The speech is **delivered from memory**. Topics range from country-specific issues to regional concerns to foreign policy — conflicts between nations, economic challenges in developing countries, new leadership across the globe, trade, and international institutions.

A strong IX speech provides critical thinking and perspective on an issue of contemporary significance. The student must address the question **as worded on the draw slip** and support a clear position with analysis and evidence. Extempers who explain *what* is happening — and, crucially, *why* — are favored by judges, especially since many judges and audience members may know less about a specific country or region than the speaker. Clarity is vital: foreign terms, institutions, and figures (a particular currency, a regional bloc, a head of state) should be placed in context, not assumed.

**Speech Structure (standard Extemp outline):**
1. **Introduction**: Attention-getting hook → recitation of the **Question** and the student's **Answer** to it → **Thesis** → **Preview** of the main points
2. **Body**: 2–3 major points (or two major points with sub-points), each roughly equal in length, with:
   - Sub-claim
   - Evidence (with cited sources — name and date)
   - Explanation / impact ("why this matters")
3. **Conclusion**: Restate the Question and Answer → Review of the major points → Clincher

**Key rules to know:**
- Student draws **three questions** and chooses **one** to answer
- **30 minutes** of preparation time
- Students may use their pre-gathered **files** (paper print-outs organized by topic, or electronic on a laptop/tablet) during prep
- **Internet use during prep is permitted at the National Tournament** (and at some other tournaments — check tournament rules)
- The speech is **delivered from memory**; many tournaments prohibit consulting notes during the speech, so structure and evidence must be memorized during prep
- Time limit: **7 minutes** (judges give time signals during the speech to indicate time remaining)
- Source name and date are the **minimum** citation requirement; sometimes additional source credibility is expected
- A student must **never watch the speakers before them** (they may watch those who speak after)

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Direct Answer to the Question
**What to evaluate:** Does the speech answer the *specific* question as worded on the draw slip? Is the answer (the thesis) stated clearly and early — in the introduction, not built to suspensefully at the end?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Answers the exact question directly; clear, defensible position stated upfront in the Q&A; stays on the question throughout |
| **Good** | Answers the question; position present but takes a while to arrive, or drifts toward the general region/topic rather than the precise wording |
| **Needs Work** | Vague, avoided, or non-answer; speaks to the broad topic but never takes a position; or answers a different question than the one drawn |

---

### 2. Analysis & Reasoning
**What to evaluate:** Beyond stating facts, does the student explain *why* things are happening and what follows? Do they connect evidence to the thesis, weigh causes and effects, and engage the complexity of an international issue — including historical context and cross-border dynamics?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Explains cause-and-effect and significance; links every point back to the thesis; frames the issue with regional or historical context; acknowledges complexity or counterarguments; offers genuine insight, not just a summary of news |
| **Good** | Adequate reasoning; evidence is mostly connected to the argument; some moments of real analysis amid description |
| **Needs Work** | Speech is a list of facts or a news recap; "so what?" never answered; no clear chain of reasoning from evidence to conclusion |

---

### 3. Evidence & Source Citation
**What to evaluate:** Are claims backed by specific, credible, current, and varied sources? Does the student cite **source name and date** orally? In IX, expect international and foreign-affairs outlets, and a healthy mix of US-based and international sources.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Multiple credible, recent sources cited by name *and* date; sources are varied (e.g., Council on Foreign Relations, BBC, Foreign Affairs, Foreign Policy, The Economist, Financial Times, Asia Times, Jerusalem Post); blends US and international perspectives; evidence is specific and matches the point it supports |
| **Good** | Sources cited and credible; some attributions vague or missing a date; evidence supports the points but lacks variety or leans on a single outlet |
| **Needs Work** | Little or no citation; "studies show" / "experts say" without attribution; outdated or general-knowledge claims only |

---

### 4. Organization & Structure
**What to evaluate:** Does the speech follow the Extemp architecture — intro with Q&A and preview, 2–3 balanced body points, conclusion that restates the answer and reviews points? Are transitions and signposting clear?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Clean Q&A → thesis → preview; 2–3 clearly-labeled, roughly equal body points; smooth signposted transitions; conclusion mirrors the intro and reviews the points |
| **Good** | Structure present and followable; preview used; transitions functional but occasionally abrupt; points slightly unbalanced |
| **Needs Work** | Hard to follow; no clear preview; points blur together or are lopsided; conclusion abrupt or missing |

---

### 5. Vocal Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Rate, volume, articulation, and use of pause. Given limited prep, judge charitably — but clarity and confidence still matter. Note that foreign names, places, and terms should be pronounced with reasonable confidence and consistency.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Conversational and confident; pace lets ideas land; meaningful pauses; clear articulation; foreign names and terms handled smoothly; voice conveys engagement with the topic |
| **Good** | Mostly clear; some filler words ("um," "like," "so"), often at transitions; minor rushing or monotone stretches; occasional hesitation on unfamiliar names |
| **Needs Work** | Excessive fillers; pace too fast or halting; mumbled or monotone; foreign terms stumbled over repeatedly; sounds like memorized text being retrieved rather than thought through |

---

### 6. Physical Delivery & Fluency
**What to evaluate:** Eye contact, posture, gestures, and composure. Does the student look like they are thinking through the argument in real time, or reciting?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Consistent eye contact distributed across the room; purposeful natural gestures; poised, controlled stance; recovers smoothly from any stumble |
| **Good** | Generally good eye contact with occasional drift up/away when retrieving information; some gestures; minor nervous habits |
| **Needs Work** | Eye contact mostly lost; rigid or fidgety; distracting habits (swaying, pacing); visible memory lapses that derail the flow |

---

### 7. Overall Effect / Persuasiveness
**What to evaluate:** Did the speech advance a clear, well-supported position on the question? Did you leave intellectually satisfied — with an answer and a perspective on a corner of the world you may not have known well?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Left with a clear answer and a compelling, well-evidenced argument; the student's command of the international issue and its context was evident |
| **Good** | Position was clear and the argument solid; research and engagement present but the analysis didn't fully land |
| **Needs Work** | Unclear position; felt like a survey of the topic rather than an argument; little sense the student grasped the question's significance or global context |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **Does the speech answer the *exact* question?** This is the most fundamental criterion. A polished speech that answers a vaguely-related topic — not the question on the slip — should rank low. Re-read the question wording before you score.
- **Is the answer stated early?** Extemp uses the Q&A format: restate the question and answer it directly in the introduction, *then* thesis and preview. Building to the answer suspense-style is a structural weakness.
- **Source credibility, currency, and variety.** In IX, expect international-focused sources — Council on Foreign Relations, BBC, Foreign Affairs, Foreign Policy, The Economist, The Financial Times, Asia Times, Jerusalem Post, the Wall Street Journal, and the New York Times. The strongest speeches blend US-based and international outlets. Name *and* date are the baseline; "analysts say" or "according to reports" is weak.
- **Context for the unfamiliar.** Flag foreign terms, institutions, currencies, and figures used without explanation. A judge or observer may not know a particular nation's currency, party, or regional bloc unless the speaker provides that context — clarity for the audience is part of the event.
- **Filler words.** Common in a limited-prep event, especially at transitions. Note if excessive, but stay charitable.
- **Internet at Nationals.** Students may use the internet during prep at the National Tournament, so their evidence should be current and well-sourced. Hold sourcing to a higher standard there.
- **Time.** 7-minute maximum. You should give time signals during the speech. Note significant overages on the ballot. Very short speeches generally read as underdeveloped (check tab for tournament-specific rules).
- **Do not coach or intervene.** Give the time signals expected of you, but never prompt content during the round.

## Sample Judge Comments

### Direct Answer to the Question

**Strong:**
- answered exact question in intro
- clear position held throughout
- proper Q&A format

**Average:**
- answer arrives too late
- drifts to broad region/topic

**Needs Work:**
- no clear answer given
- restate question, then answer
- anchor thesis upfront

---

### Analysis & Reasoning

**Strong:**
- explains why, not just what
- historical/regional context adds depth
- addresses counterarguments
- genuine analysis, not recap

**Average:**
- evidence not always explained
- answer "so what?" each time

**Needs Work:**
- reads like a news report
- interpret events, don't list them
- bridge evidence to thesis

---

### Evidence & Source Citation

**Strong:**
- specific, credible, current sources
- name and date each claim
- blends international and US outlets

**Average:**
- tighten vague attributions
- always add the date
- vary your sources

**Needs Work:**
- needs oral citations
- attribute every factual claim
- name and date are minimum

---

### Organization & Structure

**Strong:**
- clean Q&A and preview
- balanced, signposted body points
- conclusion restates and reviews

**Average:**
- abrupt transitions between points
- balance time across points

**Needs Work:**
- structure not visible
- add a clear preview
- label body points distinctly

---

### Vocal Delivery

**Strong:**
- conversational and well-paced
- pauses let ideas land
- foreign names handled smoothly

**Average:**
- filler words at transitions
- rehearse transition language
- practice harder place-names aloud

**Needs Work:**
- pace too fast to absorb
- frequent fillers signal uncertainty
- slow down, rehearse foreign terms

---

### Physical Delivery & Fluency

**Strong:**
- eye contact across the room
- gestures from argument, not nerves
- composed recovery from stumble

**Average:**
- gaze drifts when retrieving
- anchor eyes on audience
- more intentional gestures

**Needs Work:**
- visible memory lapses break flow
- eye contact stuck one spot
- drill structure, distribute gaze

---

### Overall Effect / Persuasiveness

**Strong:**
- answered the exact question
- current, varied international sourcing
- reasoned with regional context
- poised, compelling delivery

**Average:**
- clear position, good research
- push the analysis deeper

**Needs Work:**
- structured foundation in place
- answer the precise question
- explain why evidence matters

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] Answered the *exact* question as worded on the draw slip
- [ ] Q&A format used — answer stated early in the intro, not at the end
- [ ] Clear thesis and preview of main points in the introduction
- [ ] 2–3 balanced body points, each with claim + evidence + analysis
- [ ] Oral citations with source **name and date**; credible and current
- [ ] Sources are varied — blends international and US-based outlets
- [ ] Analysis explains *why* — cause, effect, significance, and regional/historical context
- [ ] Clear signposted transitions between points
- [ ] Conclusion restates the question and answer, reviews the points
- [ ] Foreign terms, institutions, and figures placed in context for the audience
- [ ] Delivered from memory (no notes during the speech, per tournament rules)
- [ ] Within the 7-minute time limit (time signals given)
