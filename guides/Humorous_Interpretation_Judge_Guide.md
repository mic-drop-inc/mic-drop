# Humorous Interpretation (HI) — Judge Prep Guide

> **High School & Middle School** | Max 10 minutes | No props or costumes | Published work | Comedy focus

## Event Overview

Humorous Interpretation is the comedic counterpart to DI. Students perform a cutting from a **published work** (play, screenplay, novel, short story) with the primary goal of making the audience **laugh** — through character, timing, physicality, and wit. Great HI is technically demanding: it requires sharp timing, bold character choices, and the physical control to execute comedy consistently.

**Key principle:** The humor must be **motivated** — it arises from character, situation, and truth, not from random mugging or shock value. Tasteful, character-driven humor is the mark of excellent HI.

**Same dramatic arc as DI:**
- Teaser → Introduction → Exposition → Inciting Incident → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action

**What makes HI distinct:**
- The material and performance must be genuinely funny
- High physical energy and bold choices are expected
- Comedic timing is a distinct, evaluable skill
- Characters tend toward heightened, exaggerated versions of reality

---

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Literature Selection & Cutting Quality
**What to evaluate:** Is the source material genuinely funny? Is the cutting well-structured with a comedic arc? Does it avoid mean-spirited, offensive, or shock-value humor?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Published work with real comedic merit; cutting has a clear comedic arc; humor is character-driven and tasteful; story has a beginning, middle, and end |
| **Good** | Solid selection; generally funny; minor arc issues |
| **Needs Work** | Material isn't funny; humor is inappropriate, offensive, or random; cutting lacks structure; punchlines fall flat in context |

---

### 2. Introduction
**What to evaluate:** Does the intro name the work and author? Does it establish the comedic premise? Is it itself entertaining?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Names work/author; sets up comedic premise; may itself get a laugh; transitions smoothly into teaser and body |
| **Good** | Required info present; intro is functional but doesn't add to the comedy |
| **Needs Work** | Missing work title or author; intro is too long or kills momentum; over-explains the jokes |

---

### 3. Characterization & Comedic Character Work
**What to evaluate:** Are characters distinct and consistently realized? Do they commit fully to their comic premise? Are choices specific and motivated, or generic and lazy?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Each character has a distinct, committed comedic essence; characters are exaggerated but grounded in truth; transitions are crisp; no character breaks |
| **Good** | Characters generally distinguishable; good commitment most of the time; some generic choices |
| **Needs Work** | Characters blur together; comedic choices feel unmotivated or random; performer "winks" at the audience (breaks character to acknowledge the joke) |

---

### 4. Comedic Timing
**What to evaluate:** This is the signature skill of HI. Does the student know when to pause, when to rush, when to let a moment breathe? Timing is the difference between a joke landing and dying.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Pauses before punchlines consistently; reads the room and adjusts; silence is used for effect; jokes land more often than not |
| **Good** | Generally good timing; some moments rushed or held too long; most jokes land |
| **Needs Work** | Rushes through punchlines; doesn't pause for laughter; plows through even when audience isn't responding |

---

### 5. Physical Performance / Physicality
**What to evaluate:** HI rewards bold, committed physical choices. Use of the body — movement, facial expression, physical gags — is central to the event.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | High physical energy; movement is fully committed and controlled; physical gags are precisely executed; facial expressions are vivid and readable |
| **Good** | Good physicality; some commitment issues; physical choices are mostly clear |
| **Needs Work** | Minimal physicality; performer "narrates" comedy without embodying it; lack of facial expression; physical choices feel safe or half-committed |

---

### 6. Vocal Performance
**What to evaluate:** Vocal variety, distinct character voices, timing in vocal delivery, and ability to modulate for comedic effect.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Distinct voices for each character; vocal dynamics serve the comedy; knows when to go big and when to underplay |
| **Good** | Generally clear; some vocal variety; characters distinguishable vocally |
| **Needs Work** | Monotone; characters sound alike; volume level doesn't vary; not using voice as a comedic tool |

---

### 7. Overall Humor / Effect
**What to evaluate:** Did the performance make you laugh? Was it a cohesive comedic experience? Was the humor tasteful and motivated?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Genuinely funny; audience clearly responded; performance built comedically; humor felt earned and character-driven |
| **Good** | Funny in parts; solid comedic experience; some moments didn't land but most did |
| **Needs Work** | Didn't generate much laughter; humor was inappropriate, offensive, or entirely random; felt more like drama with some jokes than genuine HI |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **Is it actually funny?** The most fundamental criterion. Be honest. If nothing made you laugh or smile, that's a significant weakness.
- **Tasteful vs. offensive humor:** HI should be appropriate for all audiences. Note any humor that relies on stereotypes, inappropriate sexual content, or shock value — this can be penalized.
- **Off-stage focus:** Like DI, character dialogue should use off-stage focal points, not direct eye contact with the audience or judge.
- **No props or costumes:** Any physical items used as props are a rules violation.
- **Published source:** Must be from a published work (not student-written). If the piece seems original or too tailored to be from a real publication, note it.
- **Character breaks:** If the performer laughs at their own joke or "winks" at the judge to signal a punchline, that's a break in character — note it.
- **"Mugging":** Excessive over-the-top physical comedy without motivation is a common HI weakness. Note if the comedy feels desperate rather than earned.

## Sample Judge Comments

### Literature Selection & Cutting

**Strong:**
- excellent comedic source material
- humor grounded in character
- satisfying comedic arc preserved

**Average:**
- genuinely funny material
- escalate comedic stakes per scene

**Needs Work:**
- reads dramatic, not comedic
- humor sporadic, not sustained
- pick comedy-driven source

---

### Introduction

**Strong:**
- efficient and funny intro
- names work and author
- got a laugh early

**Average:**
- required info present
- doesn't yet tap the comedy

**Needs Work:**
- intro ran too long
- explained jokes before they happened
- set up premise, not punchlines

---

### Characterization & Comedic Character Work

**Strong:**
- brilliantly realized characters
- recognizable physical signatures
- no character breaks

**Average:**
- characters distinguishable, mostly committed
- sharpen secondary character signatures

**Needs Work:**
- characters feel too similar
- commit fully to comedic essence
- give each exaggerated signature

---

### Comedic Timing

**Strong:**
- exceptional comedic timing
- pauses before punchlines
- adjusts to the audience

**Average:**
- mostly solid timing
- occasionally rushes punchlines

**Needs Work:**
- jokes arrive a beat fast
- pause before the payoff
- record yourself to find gaps

---

### Physical Performance

**Strong:**
- outstanding, committed physicality
- precisely executed physical gags
- whole body as comic instrument

**Average:**
- active and engaged physicality
- commit even more fully

**Needs Work:**
- somewhat reserved physically
- comedy lives in the body
- bolder physical choices needed

---

### Vocal Performance

**Strong:**
- distinct, recognizable character voices
- sharp timing on punch lines
- pitch, pace, volume as tools

**Average:**
- solid, mostly distinguishable voices
- widen contrast between characters

**Needs Work:**
- characters sound too alike
- build a different voice each
- mark and land punch lines

---

### Overall Effect

**Strong:**
- genuinely funny performance
- audience laughed consistently
- comedy built to a high note

**Average:**
- fun, real comedic moments
- choreograph escalating laughs

**Needs Work:**
- needs more sustained comedy
- long stretches where nothing lands
- find comedic premise each scene

---

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] Teaser before introduction
- [ ] Introduction names work and author
- [ ] Material is genuinely funny (not just attempted funny)
- [ ] No props or costumes (rules violation if present)
- [ ] Off-stage focus maintained (character dialogue not directed at audience)
- [ ] Distinct, committed characters
- [ ] Comedic timing with appropriate pauses
- [ ] High physical energy and bold physicality
- [ ] No character breaks or "winking" at audience
- [ ] Humor is tasteful and character-motivated (not offensive/shock)
- [ ] Within 10-minute time limit
