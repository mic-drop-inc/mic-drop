# United States Extemporaneous Speaking (USX) — Judge Prep Guide

> **High School Event** | 30 minutes prep | Max 7 minutes speech | Delivered from memory | Focus: U.S. domestic affairs

## Event Overview

In United States Extemporaneous Speaking (USX), a student is presented with a **choice of three questions** related to current events in the U.S. and selects **one**. With **30 minutes** to prepare, they build and deliver a **seven-minute speech** answering the chosen question. During prep, students may consult research (their "files") they have gathered beforehand, and at the National Tournament they may use the internet. The speech is **delivered from memory**. Topics range from political matters to economic concerns to U.S. foreign policy.

A strong USX speech provides critical thinking and perspective on an issue of contemporary significance. The student must address the question **as worded on the draw slip** and support a clear position with analysis and evidence. Extempers who explain *what* is happening — and, crucially, *why* — are favored by judges, especially since many judges and audience members may know less about the specific topic than the speaker. Clarity is vital: specialized terms (Super PACs, the debt ceiling, the filibuster) should be placed in context, not assumed.

**Speech Structure (standard Extemp outline):**
1. **Introduction**: Attention-getting hook → recitation of the **Question** and the student's **Answer** to it → **Thesis** → **Preview** of the main points
2. **Body**: 2–3 major points (or two major points with sub-points), each roughly equal in length, with:
   - Sub-claim
   - Evidence (with cited sources — name and date)
   - Explanation / impact ("why this matters")
3. **Conclusion**: Restate the Question and Answer → Review of the major points → Clincher

**Key rules to know:**
- Student draws **three questions** and chooses **one** to answer
- **30 minutes** of preparation time
- Students may use their pre-gathered **files** (paper print-outs or electronic on a laptop/tablet) during prep
- **Internet use during prep is permitted at the National Tournament** (and at some other tournaments — check tournament rules)
- The speech is **delivered from memory**; many tournaments prohibit consulting notes during the speech, so structure and evidence must be memorized during prep
- Time limit: **7 minutes** (judges give time signals during the speech to indicate time remaining)
- Source name and date are the **minimum** citation requirement; sometimes additional source credibility is expected
- A student must **never watch the speakers before them** (they may watch those who speak after)

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Direct Answer to the Question
**What to evaluate:** Does the speech answer the *specific* question as worded on the draw slip? Is the answer (the thesis) stated clearly and early — in the introduction, not built to suspensefully at the end?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Answers the exact question directly; clear, defensible position stated upfront in the Q&A; stays on the question throughout |
| **Good** | Answers the question; position present but takes a while to arrive, or drifts toward the general topic rather than the precise wording |
| **Needs Work** | Vague, avoided, or non-answer; speaks to the broad topic but never takes a position; or answers a different question than the one drawn |

---

### 2. Analysis & Reasoning
**What to evaluate:** Beyond stating facts, does the student explain *why* things are happening and what follows? Do they connect evidence to the thesis, weigh causes and effects, and engage the complexity of a domestic issue?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Explains cause-and-effect and significance; links every point back to the thesis; acknowledges complexity or counterarguments; offers genuine insight, not just a summary of news |
| **Good** | Adequate reasoning; evidence is mostly connected to the argument; some moments of real analysis amid description |
| **Needs Work** | Speech is a list of facts or a news recap; "so what?" never answered; no clear chain of reasoning from evidence to conclusion |

---

### 3. Evidence & Source Citation
**What to evaluate:** Are claims backed by specific, credible, current, and varied sources? Does the student cite **source name and date** orally? In USX, expect domestic-focused outlets and think tanks.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Multiple credible, recent sources cited by name *and* date; sources are varied (e.g., NYT, Brookings, Bloomberg, Congressional Research Service, Pew, CBO); evidence is specific and matches the point it supports |
| **Good** | Sources cited and credible; some attributions vague or missing a date; evidence supports the points but lacks variety |
| **Needs Work** | Little or no citation; "studies show" / "experts say" without attribution; outdated or general-knowledge claims only |

---

### 4. Organization & Structure
**What to evaluate:** Does the speech follow the Extemp architecture — intro with Q&A and preview, 2–3 balanced body points, conclusion that restates the answer and reviews points? Are transitions and signposting clear?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Clean Q&A → thesis → preview; 2–3 clearly-labeled, roughly equal body points; smooth signposted transitions; conclusion mirrors the intro and reviews the points |
| **Good** | Structure present and followable; preview used; transitions functional but occasionally abrupt; points slightly unbalanced |
| **Needs Work** | Hard to follow; no clear preview; points blur together or are lopsided; conclusion abrupt or missing |

---

### 5. Vocal Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Rate, volume, articulation, and use of pause. Given limited prep, judge charitably — but clarity and confidence still matter.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Conversational and confident; pace lets ideas land; meaningful pauses; clear articulation; voice conveys engagement with the topic |
| **Good** | Mostly clear; some filler words ("um," "like," "so"), often at transitions; minor rushing or monotone stretches |
| **Needs Work** | Excessive fillers; pace too fast or halting; mumbled or monotone; sounds like memorized text being retrieved rather than thought through |

---

### 6. Physical Delivery & Fluency
**What to evaluate:** Eye contact, posture, gestures, and composure. Does the student look like they are thinking through the argument in real time, or reciting?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Consistent eye contact distributed across the room; purposeful natural gestures; poised, controlled stance; recovers smoothly from any stumble |
| **Good** | Generally good eye contact with occasional drift up/away when retrieving information; some gestures; minor nervous habits |
| **Needs Work** | Eye contact mostly lost; rigid or fidgety; distracting habits (swaying, pacing); visible memory lapses that derail the flow |

---

### 7. Overall Effect / Persuasiveness
**What to evaluate:** Did the speech advance a clear, well-supported position on the question? Did you leave intellectually satisfied — with an answer and a perspective?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Left with a clear answer and a compelling, well-evidenced argument; the student's command of the domestic issue was evident |
| **Good** | Position was clear and the argument solid; research and engagement present but the analysis didn't fully land |
| **Needs Work** | Unclear position; felt like a survey of the topic rather than an argument; little sense the student grasped the question's significance |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **Does the speech answer the *exact* question?** This is the most fundamental criterion. A polished speech that answers a vaguely-related topic — not the question on the slip — should rank low. Re-read the question wording before you score.
- **Is the answer stated early?** Extemp uses the Q&A format: restate the question and answer it directly in the introduction, *then* thesis and preview. Building to the answer suspense-style is a structural weakness.
- **Source credibility, currency, and variety.** In USX, expect domestic-focused sources — New York Times, Brookings Institution, Bloomberg, The Economist, The Financial Times, Congressional Research Service, Pew, CBO, Federal Reserve statements. Name *and* date are the baseline; "scientists say" or "according to studies" is weak.
- **Filler words.** Common in a limited-prep event, especially at transitions. Note if excessive, but stay charitable.
- **Internet at Nationals.** Students may use the internet during prep at the National Tournament, so their evidence should be current and well-sourced. Hold sourcing to a higher standard there.
- **Jargon without context.** Flag specialized terms (Super PAC, debt ceiling, reconciliation, the filibuster) used without explanation — clarity for the audience is part of the event.
- **Time.** 7-minute maximum. You should give time signals during the speech. Note significant overages on the ballot. Very short speeches generally read as underdeveloped (check tab for tournament-specific rules).
- **Do not coach or intervene.** Give the time signals expected of you, but never prompt content during the round.

## Sample Judge Comments

### Direct Answer to the Question

**Strong:**
- answered exact question in intro
- clear position stated upfront
- held position throughout

**Average:**
- spoke to broad topic, not wording
- answer arrived late
- check position against exact wording

**Needs Work:**
- answer unclear after full speech
- restate question, then answer directly
- anchor everything to upfront answer

---

### Analysis & Reasoning

**Strong:**
- explained why, not just what
- traced implications forward
- addressed strongest objection

**Average:**
- evidence solid but unexplained
- answer "so what?" after each point
- connect evidence to argument

**Needs Work:**
- reads like news recap
- interpret facts, don't just list
- build bridge from evidence to thesis

---

### Evidence & Source Citation

**Strong:**
- specific, credible, current sources
- name and date for each claim
- nice variety of outlets/think tanks

**Average:**
- tighten vague attributions
- add dates to citations
- vary your sources

**Needs Work:**
- needs oral citations
- source name and date are minimum
- claims read as opinion without attribution

---

### Organization & Structure

**Strong:**
- clean Q&A and clear preview
- balanced, signposted body points
- conclusion mirrors the intro

**Average:**
- abrupt transitions between points
- second point ran short
- signpost and balance the points

**Needs Work:**
- structure not visible
- no clear preview
- make the Extemp skeleton obvious

---

### Vocal Delivery

**Strong:**
- conversational and well-paced
- pauses let key ideas land
- sounded like real-time reasoning

**Average:**
- clear and easy to follow
- fillers cluster at transitions
- rehearse transition language aloud

**Needs Work:**
- pace too fast to absorb
- frequent fillers feel uncertain
- slow down, rehearse connective phrases

---

### Physical Delivery & Fluency

**Strong:**
- eye contact across the room
- gestures arise from argument
- composed recovery from lost place

**Average:**
- gaze drifts when retrieving info
- anchor eyes back on audience
- make gestures more intentional

**Needs Work:**
- visible memory lapses break flow
- eye contact stuck on one spot
- drill structure to free up delivery

---

### Overall Effect / Persuasiveness

**Strong:**
- answered exact question with poise
- current, varied sourcing
- left with clear answer and perspective

**Average:**
- clear position, good research
- push the analysis deeper
- change how audience thinks, not just knows

**Needs Work:**
- foundation is here
- answer the precise question
- explain why evidence matters

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] Answered the *exact* question as worded on the draw slip
- [ ] Q&A format used — answer stated early in the intro, not at the end
- [ ] Clear thesis and preview of main points in the introduction
- [ ] 2–3 balanced body points, each with claim + evidence + analysis
- [ ] Oral citations with source **name and date**; credible and current
- [ ] Sources are varied (multiple outlets / think tanks)
- [ ] Analysis explains *why* — cause, effect, and significance
- [ ] Clear signposted transitions between points
- [ ] Conclusion restates the question and answer, reviews the points
- [ ] Specialized terms placed in context for the audience
- [ ] Delivered from memory (no notes during the speech, per tournament rules)
- [ ] Within the 7-minute time limit (time signals given)
