# Expository Speaking — Judge Prep Guide

> **Middle School Event** | Max 5 minutes | No visual aids at Nationals | Self-written informative speech

## Event Overview

Expository is a **middle school** public speaking event where students deliver an original, self-written speech that **informs** the audience about a topic of their choice. The goal is to educate — not persuade or advocate. At NSDA Nationals specifically, **no visual aids** are permitted (visual aids are allowed at regular-season tournaments but not Nationals).

**Structure:**
- **Introduction** (30–45 sec): Hook, thesis, and preview of main points
- **Body** (3:00–3:15): 2–3 main points with supporting evidence and explanation
- **Conclusion** (15–30 sec): Restatement of thesis, review of points, call to remember/act

**Key traits of strong Expository speakers:**
- Articulate and clear
- Process-oriented (explains HOW things work)
- Inquisitive mindset — genuine curiosity about the topic
- Engaging delivery
- Personable and conversational, not lecturing

## Judging Criteria

### 1. Topic Selection & Originality
**What to evaluate:** Is the topic interesting and appropriate for an informative speech? Is it fresh and specific, or overdone? Is the scope manageable in 5 minutes?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Surprising, narrow, and genuinely interesting topic; audience learns something new; scope is right for the time limit |
| **Good** | Solid topic; some over-familiarity; mostly well-scoped |
| **Needs Work** | Overly broad (e.g., "climate change"), too shallow, or so niche it loses the audience; topic feels like a report |

---

### 2. Content / Research Quality
**What to evaluate:** Are claims supported with evidence? Are sources cited orally? Is the information accurate and substantive? Is the 150-word direct quotation limit respected?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Well-researched; oral citations present; evidence is specific and compelling; explains cause, process, or meaning — not just facts |
| **Good** | Evidence present; some citations; explanation is adequate but could go deeper |
| **Needs Work** | Unsupported claims; no oral citations; information feels thin or vague; relies on general knowledge |

---

### 3. Organization & Structure
**What to evaluate:** Is there a clear introduction, body, and conclusion? Are transitions smooth? Does the structure serve comprehension?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Clear thesis with preview; organized body with distinct main points; smooth transitions; conclusion ties everything together |
| **Good** | Structure present; transitions somewhat formulaic; conclusion restates without adding meaning |
| **Needs Work** | Hard to identify main points; no clear thesis or preview; transitions are abrupt or absent; no real conclusion |

---

### 4. Language / Word Choice
**What to evaluate:** Is the language clear, vivid, and appropriate for the topic? Does the student use language to illuminate rather than obscure?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Precise language; uses analogies, examples, and imagery to make complex ideas accessible; avoids jargon without explanation |
| **Good** | Generally clear; occasional vague or filler language; some good word choices |
| **Needs Work** | Heavy jargon without explanation; vague or repetitive language; filler words (like, um, basically) distract |

---

### 5. Vocal Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Rate, volume, pitch, articulation, and use of pause. Does the voice keep the audience engaged?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Dynamic and varied; pacing is appropriate; key ideas are emphasized; pauses are used for effect |
| **Good** | Mostly clear and audible; some variety; occasional rushing or monotone |
| **Needs Work** | Monotone or too fast; difficult to understand; no meaningful variation in pace or emphasis |

---

### 6. Physical Delivery
**What to evaluate:** Eye contact, gestures, posture, movement, and facial expression.

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Natural eye contact with the whole room; gestures reinforce content; confident posture; facial expression matches content |
| **Good** | Generally good eye contact; some gestures; occasional nervous habits |
| **Needs Work** | Reads notes or looks away frequently; minimal gestures; rigid or nervous physical presence |

---

### 7. Overall Effect / Educational Impact
**What to evaluate:** Did the audience learn something? Was the speech engaging from start to finish?

| Level | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| **Excellent** | Genuinely informative and memorable; audience leaves knowing something they didn't before; student's enthusiasm is contagious |
| **Good** | Educational value is there; some moments of genuine engagement |
| **Needs Work** | Feels like a textbook recitation; audience unlikely to retain the information |

---

## Things to Watch For as a Judge

- **No visual aids at NSDA Nationals:** If a student uses a poster, prop, or any visual aid, note it on the ballot — it's a rules violation at this tournament.
- **This is INFORMATIVE, not persuasive:** If the speech makes a value judgment or argues for a position (e.g., "we should do X"), that's more OO than Expository. Note the distinction.
- **Middle school energy and age:** Adjust expectations appropriately — these are middle schoolers. Look for age-appropriate maturity and content.
- **150-word direct quotation limit:** Can't verify precisely as a judge, but if the speech seems to consist mostly of read quotations, note it.
- **Time limit:** 5 minutes max. Note any violation.
- **Oral citations:** Students should say who they're quoting or citing. "According to [source]…" or "Research by [author] found…" are standard forms.

## Sample Judge Comments

### Topic Selection & Originality

**Strong:**
- genuinely surprising topic choice
- specific focus feels fresh
- personal curiosity clearly showed

**Average:**
- common Expository topic
- drill into a more specific angle

**Needs Work:**
- topic far too broad
- each point too shallow
- pick a narrower slice for depth

---

### Content / Research Quality

**Strong:**
- thorough, well-integrated research
- explained the why and how
- oral citations gave credibility

**Average:**
- solid, accurate content
- add named studies and statistics
- push research deeper

**Needs Work:**
- relies on general knowledge
- claims lack supporting evidence
- add citations and specific data

---

### Organization & Structure

**Strong:**
- crystal-clear thesis
- smooth guiding transitions
- conclusion adds final meaning

**Average:**
- transitions a little abrupt
- add bridging sentences between points

**Needs Work:**
- main points hard to identify
- no clear preview in intro
- add numbered preview and signposts

---

### Language / Word Choice

**Strong:**
- exceptional use of analogy
- abstract ideas made accessible
- gift for the right word

**Average:**
- clear but occasionally vague
- use vivid, specific imagery

**Needs Work:**
- unexplained technical terms
- assume audience knows nothing
- cut filler words (like, um, basically)

---

### Vocal Delivery

**Strong:**
- pacing let information land
- key terms emphasized naturally
- purposeful, audible pauses

**Average:**
- good volume and articulation
- add more dynamic variation

**Needs Work:**
- consistent pace throughout
- no vocal emphasis on key moments
- mark important moments in script

---

### Physical Delivery

**Strong:**
- confident, natural presence
- eye contact across the room
- gestures arose organically

**Average:**
- eye contact favors one side
- connect with all corners
- add intention behind gestures

**Needs Work:**
- limited eye contact
- looks above audience or down
- memorize material to look up

---

### Overall Effect

**Strong:**
- genuinely informative and enjoyable
- left with clear understanding
- contagious enthusiasm for subject

**Average:**
- solid content
- make your enthusiasm more visible
- show why you find it fascinating

**Needs Work:**
- information present but flat
- be a passionate guide, not narrator
- let genuine curiosity lead

---

## Quick Reference Checklist

- [ ] No visual aids (Nationals rule — violation if present)
- [ ] Topic is informative, not persuasive
- [ ] Clear thesis and preview in introduction
- [ ] Body has 2–3 distinct main points
- [ ] Evidence supported with oral citations
- [ ] Smooth transitions between points
- [ ] Conclusion ties the speech together
- [ ] Vocal variety and clear articulation
- [ ] Strong eye contact and physical presence
- [ ] Within 5-minute time limit
